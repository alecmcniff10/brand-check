360 Deals: Considerations For An Artist's Long-Term Income

The way artists make money in today’s music industry has changed drastically. Album sales no longer dominate revenue streams. Instead, different buckets like touring, merchandise, streaming, endorsements, and licensing often represent a larger share of an artist’s income.

Alec McNiff · 3 min read

The way artists make money in today’s music industry has changed drastically. Album sales no longer dominate revenue streams. Instead, different buckets like touring, merchandise, streaming, endorsements, and licensing often represent a larger share of an artist’s income. In response, many record labels offer 360 deals: contracts that give the label a percentage of nearly every revenue stream an artist generates. This article analyzes the effects of 360 deals on an artist’s long-term earnings. 

What Is a 360 Deal?

A 360 deal is a type of record contract where the label receives a portion of the artist's income in multiple areas beyond just music sales. This may include touring, merchandise, publishing, sponsorships, and even digital revenue. Traditionally, labels earned primarily from album sales. However, the decline in physical sales and the rise of streaming prompted the adoption of these comprehensive “360” agreements. Although percentages vary by artist, labels may take anywhere from 10% to 30% of non-recording income streams.

Benefits of 360 Deals

For emerging artists, 360 deals can provide significant advantages. First, they can give artists access to additional resources, as labels often provide marketing, tour support, and brand development that might otherwise be out of reach. Likewise, labels may offer increased financial support, such as higher upfront advances to cover recording costs, tours, and promotional campaigns. Greater investment in the artist and leveraging the label’s expertise and connections can result in more exposure and faster career growth.

Risks and Drawbacks

Despite the benefits, 360 deals are not without trade-offs. Giving a cut of every income stream can significantly reduce long-term earnings, particularly from tours and merchandise, which are often highly profitable. Additionally, an artist often forfeits autonomy in exchange for a 360 deal, as the label exercises more influence on decisions about touring, partnerships, or merchandising. And for mid-tier or established artists, the label’s percentage may outweigh the benefits, especially if the artist’s brand grows stronger independently.

Negotiating a 360 Deal

For artists considering a 360 deal, legal guidance becomes even more important. Artists should pay attention to the scope of revenue streams the label wants to participate in, since not all streams need to be included. For example, many artists successfully negotiate to exclude merchandise, digital revenue, or other high-value areas. Recoupment clauses also deserve careful scrutiny, as they determine how advances are recovered across multiple income sources and can significantly affect long-term earnings. Additionally, counsel should evaluate the duration and territory of the agreement to ensure the deal doesn’t bind the artist to restrictive terms for an unreasonable period. For some artists, a partial 360 deal, where the label participates in only select revenue streams, can strike a better balance by limiting the label’s percentage to certain areas, allowing the artist to maintain full control over their most important revenue sources while still benefiting from the label’s support.

Long-Term Financial Implications

The upshot is that 360 deals can be a double-edged sword. While they offer resources and financial backing that can launch an artist’s career, they also involve sharing a significant portion of revenue. Artists must weigh the value of immediate support against the potential cost of long-term earnings. Artists with enough leverage may be able to mitigate some of the downside by retaining control over high-value streams, such as merchandise or publishing.

Conclusion

A 360 deal is not inherently bad, but it is not automatically good either. Artists should carefully evaluate their current career stage, projected growth, and the label’s track record before deciding whether a 360 deal makes sense for the artist’s short-term needs and long-term income potential.
