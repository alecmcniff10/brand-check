USPTO Trademark Registration Series: Part 5 – Publication, Opposition, and Path to Allowance/Registration

Once an application has been approved by an examining attorney (whether after initial review or following responses to office actions) it advances to publication.

Alec McNiff · 6 min read

Once an application has been approved by an examining attorney (whether after initial review or following responses to office actions) it advances to publication. This stage opens the application to potential challenges from third parties. Many applications advance from publication to registration without issue, but understanding the opposition process and post-publication requirements is essential to protecting the mark and avoiding delays. 

This article explores the path from publication to allowance or registration and highlights the key procedural and strategic considerations at each stage.

Approval for Publication in the Official Gazette

After the examining attorney determines that an application meets all statutory and procedural requirements, the USPTO approves the mark for publication. This approval typically occurs after any office actions are resolved, but timelines can vary based on USPTO workload and the application’s complexity.

The mark is then published in the Trademark Official Gazette (TMOG), a weekly online publication issued by the USPTO. Publication serves as public notice that the USPTO intends to register the mark, giving interested parties an opportunity to potentially challenge it. Publication, which is approximately one month after approval, initiates a 30-day opposition period, during which any person or entity believing they would be damaged by the registration may file a notice of opposition. If no opposition is filed, the application proceeds toward registration, though it may take an additional three to four months to receive official notification of the next steps.

The Opposition Period

The 30-day opposition window provides an opportunity for third parties to contest registration. Potential opposers must file a notice of opposition electronically through the USPTO’s Electronic System for Trademark Trials and Appeals (ESTTA), paying the required fee (which is currently $600 per class). The notice must specify grounds for opposition, such as likelihood of confusion with an existing mark, descriptiveness, or lack of distinctiveness.

Oppositions are relatively uncommon. USPTO data indicate that approximately 7,000 to 8,000 oppositions are filed annually, representing less than 2% of the hundreds of thousands of marks published each year. This low rate reflects the rigorous examination process that weeds out many conflicts earlier, but it underscores the importance of thorough pre-filing clearance searches to minimize risks.

Potential opposers may also request extensions of time to oppose, initially for 30 days (no good cause or consent required) or 90 days (with good cause shown, no consent required), to investigate or negotiate. If an opposition is filed, the process shifts to the Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB) for resolution.

Handling Oppositions and TTAB Proceedings

An opposition proceeding is an inter partes, adversarial process conducted before the Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB), an administrative tribunal within the USPTO. Although it shares structural similarities with federal court litigation, the process is generally more streamlined and cost-effective, relying primarily on written submissions, limited discovery, and briefing rather than live testimony or a full trial, unless specifically requested.

The opposer, acting as the plaintiff, must establish standing and prove valid grounds for refusal, while the applicant defends the registrability of the mark. Common grounds for opposition include priority of use, likelihood of confusion, and claims that a mark is generic or merely descriptive. Proceedings typically unfold in defined phases, including discovery, the submission of evidence during the trial period, and written briefing. Oral hearings are optional and relatively uncommon.

In practice, most oppositions resolve through settlement before a final decision is issued, often through coexistence agreements or amendments to the identification of goods or services. If the TTAB ultimately rules in favor of the opposer, the application may be refused in whole or in part, subject to the applicant’s right to appeal the decision to federal court.

Applicants facing opposition should consult a U.S.-licensed trademark attorney with experience in TTAB proceedings. While early settlements may resolve disputes for several thousand dollars, fully litigated oppositions can exceed $75,000. Strategic early resolution can significantly reduce costs. During an opposition, limited amendments to the application may be permitted with TTAB approval, though such changes may require republication of the mark.

Notice of Allowance for Intent-to-Use Applications

The path after publication diverges based on the application’s filing basis. For use-based applications (filed under Section 1(a) of the Lanham Act), if no opposition is filed or it is unsuccessful, the mark proceeds directly to registration. The USPTO issues the registration certificate shortly thereafter.

In contrast, intent-to-use (ITU) applications (filed under Section 1(b), based on a bona fide intent to use the mark) receive a Notice of Allowance (NOA) after the opposition period ends without challenge or upon resolution of any opposition. The NOA indicates that the mark is allowable but not yet registered, as actual use in commerce must be demonstrated.

Foreign-based applications (under Section 44 or 66) follow a similar path to ITU, requiring proof of use or excusable nonuse in some cases.

Filing Statement of Use or Extensions

For ITU applications, the applicant must file a Statement of Use (SOU) within six months of the NOA issuance date, submitting specimens showing the mark in actual use in commerce for the specified goods or services. The SOU must be filed via TEAS, accompanied by a fee ($150 per class) and verified under penalty of perjury. 

If use has not commenced, applicants may request extensions of time, each for an additional six months, up to five extensions total (for a maximum of 36 months from the NOA date). Each extension requires a fee of $125 per class (if filed electronically) and a statement affirming continued bona fide intent to use the mark. Extensions are not automatic and must demonstrate good cause after the first one, such as ongoing product development or market research.

Failure to file a timely SOU or extension results in abandonment. Once an SOU is accepted, the mark proceeds to registration, provided no further issues arise.

Issuance of Registration Certificate

Upon successful completion of all requirements (including surviving publication and opposition, and for ITU applications, acceptance of the SOU) the USPTO issues a registration certificate. This grants nationwide constructive notice of ownership, prima facie evidence of validity, and enhanced remedies against infringement.

Registration typically occurs three to four months after the end of the opposition period for use-based applications, or after SOU acceptance for ITU. Applicants receive the certificate electronically or by mail, and the mark is entered on the Principal Register (or Supplemental Register, if applicable).

Key Considerations During and After the Opposition Period

Several strategic factors can influence success during the publication and opposition phase:

  1. Monitoring for Oppositions: Applicants should actively monitor the Trademark Official Gazette for their publication and consider trademark watching services to identify potential opposers early. In some cases, proactive outreach can resolve concerns before a formal opposition is filed.
  1. Deadlines for SOU and Extensions: For ITU applicants, the initial six-month SOU window is strict, with extensions providing flexibility up to three years total. Careful deadline tracking is essential, as missed deadlines generally result in abandonment with no meaningful opportunity for revival.
  1. Limited Post-NOA Amendments: After issuance of the NOA (or post-publication for use-based), amendments are restricted. Broadening goods/services or changing the mark is generally prohibited. Narrowing or clarifications may be allowed, but it may require republication and restarting the opposition period.
  1. Risks of Third-Party Challenges: Beyond opposition, registered marks remain vulnerable to cancellation proceedings at the TTAB for up to five years (or indefinitely for certain grounds like fraud). Pre-filing clearance and ongoing monitoring help mitigate these risks.
  1. Differences for Use-Based vs. Intent-to-Use: Use-based applications offer quicker registration but require upfront proof of use, limiting flexibility for new brands. ITU provides time to develop use but adds SOU requirements and extension fees, with the risk of abandonment if use never materializes. The appropriate filing type depends on business timing: intent-to-use applications work well for planned launches, while use-based applications are better suited for marks already in commercial use.

Conclusion

The publication and opposition phases represent the final hurdles to trademark registration, where public input and proof of use ensure only eligible marks receive federal protection. Although oppositions are uncommon, being prepared is essential to defending an application. By understanding timelines, filing-basis distinctions, and strategic options, applicants can navigate these stages efficiently and maximize the benefits of registration. Treating potential challenges as a normal part of the process helps position a brand for long-term success.
