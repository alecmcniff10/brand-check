"Clinically proven" without the clinic: the FTC's 2026 substantiation guidance, decoded.

What changed for influencer brand deals, what didn't, and the disclosure language your contract should already be using.

Alec R. Whitfield · 6 min read

The headline of the FTC's revised substantiation guidance is that "clinically proven" now means what a reasonable consumer thinks it means: a clinic, with a study, that proved the thing. The guidance puts the burden squarely on the advertiser, and — newly — on the creator who repeated the claim.

Most of the press coverage missed the second half of that sentence. It is the half that matters.

The new burden on creators

Under the prior framework, a creator who repeated a brand's claim could rely on the brand's substantiation, provided the creator had no reason to doubt it. The 2026 guidance keeps that rule but adds a layer: where the claim is a "health, safety, or performance" claim, the creator must affirmatively confirm that substantiation exists before publishing. A signed brand-deal questionnaire will do; a verbal assurance will not.

"Affirmative confirmation" is a higher bar than "no reason to doubt." Quietly, that is the whole change.

What every brand-deal contract should now contain

Two clauses, at minimum.

One, a substantiation representation: the brand represents that it possesses, at the time of execution, competent and reliable scientific evidence supporting any health, safety, or performance claim it asks the creator to make.

Two, a substantiation cooperation clause: the brand agrees to provide, on the creator's request and in advance of publication, a written summary of that substantiation in a form sufficient for the creator to satisfy the new affirmative-confirmation requirement.

If your existing deals have a substantiation rep but not a substantiation-cooperation clause, you have a representation the creator cannot actually use. Fix it.
