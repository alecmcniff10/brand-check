Common Defenses to Trademark Infringement 

Trademark infringement occurs when one party uses a mark that is likely to cause confusion with another’s protected trademark, potentially misleading consumers about the source, affiliation, or quality of goods or services.

Alec McNiff · 10 min read

Trademark infringement occurs when one party uses a mark that is likely to cause confusion with another’s protected trademark, potentially misleading consumers about the source, affiliation, or quality of goods or services. Under the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 1051 et seq.), the primary federal statute governing trademarks in the United States, plaintiffs must demonstrate ownership of a valid mark, prior use, and a likelihood of confusion. However, even if a plaintiff makes this prima facie showing, defendants accused of infringement are not without recourse. Several affirmative defenses can shield them from liability, allowing continued use or limiting the plaintiff’s remedies.

This article explores common defenses, including fair use (descriptive and nominative), abandonment, laches, and acquiescence. These defenses balance the need to protect trademarks with principles of fairness, competition, and equity. By examining their elements, brand owners can better protect their intellectual property and make more informed decisions in litigation or opposition proceedings.

Fair Use

The fair use defense, codified in the Lanham Act, is rooted in the idea that trademarks should not monopolize descriptive language or hinder free speech and competition. In simple terms, the defense allows limited use of another’s mark without constituting infringement. There are two main types: descriptive (or classic) fair use and nominative fair use. Both require good faith, but they apply in different contexts.

Descriptive (Classic) Fair Use

Descriptive fair use, codified in Section 33(b)(4) of the Lanham Act, permits the use of a trademark in its primary, descriptive sense rather than as a source identifier. This defense recognizes that some marks are inherently descriptive because they directly convey information about goods or services, and that owners of such marks must tolerate descriptive use by others in order to preserve fair competition. 

To establish descriptive fair use, the defendant must show: (1) the use is not as a trademark (i.e., not to indicate origin); (2) the term is used in a descriptive sense; and (3) the use is in good faith. Kelly-Brown v. Winfrey, 717 F.3d 295, 308 (2d Cir. 2013). An example of descriptive fair use comes from Sunmark, Inc. v. Ocean Spray Cranberries, Inc., where the Seventh Circuit held that a beverage company did not infringe the “Sweet-Tart” trademark, which was registered for candy, by using “sweet-tart” to describe the flavor of its juice. 64 F.3d 1055 (7th Cir. 1995). The court reasoned that the phrase conveyed taste characteristics rather than source. More recently, the Second Circuit reached the same conclusion for “red gold” as a description of watch materials despite a registered RED GOLD mark. Solid 21, Inc. v. Breitling U.S.A., Inc., 96 F.4th 265 (2d Cir. 2024).

The Supreme Court held in KP Permanent Make-Up, Inc. v. Lasting Impression I, Inc., that a defendant asserting descriptive fair use bears no burden to disprove likelihood of confusion and some degree of confusion is compatible with fair use when the defendant is using language in its ordinary descriptive sense and in good faith. 543 U.S. 111, 121-24 (2004). However, evidence of confusion is not irrelevant. Courts may consider the extent of confusion in evaluating the third element of the defense: whether the challenged use is objectively fair and undertaken in good faith. Id. at 123; JA Apparel v. Abboud, 682 F. Supp. 2d 294, 314 (S.D.N.Y. 2010). 

In short, descriptive fair use reflects the fundamental principle that ownership of a mark does not confer exclusive control over ordinary language. When a term is used to describe rather than to designate source, trademark rights give way to fair competition.

Nominative Fair Use

Nominative fair use applies when a defendant uses the plaintiff’s mark to identify the plaintiff’s goods or services, even when the purpose of that use is to market the defendant’s own goods or services. Unlike descriptive fair use, where the defendant uses the plaintiff’s mark in its ordinary descriptive sense, nominative fair use involves using the mark in its trademark sense to identify the plaintiff’s goods or services. This scenario might occur, for example, in comparative advertising, criticism, resale, and news reporting, where the mark itself is often the most efficient way to convey meaning.

Although courts almost universally accept the nominative fair use defense, a circuit split remains as to whether it is an affirmative defense or part of the likelihood of confusion analysis, and which party bears the burden of proof. In the Ninth Circuit, courts apply a three-part framework, known as the “New Kids” test, in place of the likelihood of confusion test any time the defendant has used the plaintiff’s trademark to identify the source of the plaintiff’s goods or services rather than its own. New Kids on the Block v. News Am. Publishing, Inc., 971 F.2d 302 (9th Cir. 1992); Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. v. Tabari, 610 F.3d 1171, 1182–83 (9th Cir. 2010). Under the “New Kids” test, nominative use is fair if:

  1. the product or service is not readily identifiable without use of the mark;
  2. only so much of the mark is used as reasonably necessary to identify the plaintiff’s goods or services; and
  3. the defendant does nothing that would suggest sponsorship or endorsement. Id. at 308.

It is the plaintiff’s burden to show that these elements are not satisfied by the defendant’s nominative use. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. v. Tabari, 610 F.3d 1171, 1183 (9th Cir. 2010).

Other circuits vary in approach. The Third Circuit treats nominative fair use as an affirmative defense that can defeat liability even after confusion is shown, applying a modified version of the New Kids factors. Century 21 Real Estate Corp. v. LendingTree, Inc., 425 F.3d 211, 228–32 (3d Cir. 2005). The Second Circuit incorporates nominative-use considerations into its Polaroid analysis rather than recognizing a standalone defense. Int’l Info. Sys. Sec. Certification Consortium, Inc. v. Sec. Univ., LLC, 823 F.3d 153, 167–69 (2d Cir. 2016). Still, other circuits address nominative use by applying their existing likelihood-of-confusion factors, rather than adopting a separate test.

The consistent point across circuits is that nominative use protects truthful identification, but not brand leveraging. Excessive repetition, stylized logos, or merchandising uses tend to shift the analysis from reference to appropriation. This is illustrated in Playboy Enterprises, Inc. v. Welles, where the Ninth Circuit held that a former Playmate could accurately describe herself on her website as “Playboy Playmate of the Year 1981,” but could not wallpaper the site with “PMOY ’81.” 279 F.3d 796, 803–04 (9th Cir. 2002). The court concluded that the latter went beyond what was reasonably necessary to identify her, particularly given that she had already used the full descriptive title.

The doctrine preserves the ability to speak accurately about branded goods and services, and ensures that trademark rights are not used to suppress legitimate commentary or comparison.

Abandonment

Abandonment attacks the validity of the mark itself. Under Section 45 of the Lanham Act, a mark is abandoned when its use is discontinued with intent not to resume, or when the mark becomes generic. 15 U.S.C. § 1127.

For abandonment claims based on intent not to resume use, non-use alone is insufficient. The non-use must be accompanied by intent to abandon the mark. Beech-Nut Co. v. Lorillard Co., 273 U.S. 629, 633 (1926). Nonuse for three consecutive years creates a rebuttable presumption of abandonment, shifting the burden of production to the trademark owner to demonstrate an intent to resume use in the reasonably foreseeable future. See Cumulus Media, Inc. v. Clear Channel Communs., Inc., 304 F.3d 1167, 1177 (11th Cir. 2002). However, the ultimate burden of persuasion to establish abandonment (by proving both discontinuance of use and intent not to resume) remains with the challenger. Societe de Devs. et D’Innovations des Marches Agricoles et Alimentaires-SODIMA-Union de Cooperatives Agricoles v. Int’l Yogurt Co., 662 F. Supp. 839, 845 (D. Or. 1987).

Courts assessing intent not to resume look for objective evidence such as naked licensing or the cessation of sales. See To-Ricos, Ltd. v. Productos Avicolas Del Sur, Inc., 118 F.4th 1 (1st Cir. 2024). Likewise, purported efforts to resume use must be genuine. Sporadic or token use is generally insufficient to preserve trademark rights. See, e.g., Kusek v. Family Circle, Inc., 894 F. Supp. 522 (D. Mass. 1995).

Finally, the mere failure to renew a federal registration does not, by itself, establish abandonment. Jean Patou, Inc. v. Aristocrat Products Corp., 202 U.S.P.Q. 130, 1979 WL 24833 (T.T.A.B. 1979). A registration lapse does not extinguish common-law rights where bona fide use of the mark continues. See, e.g., Crash Dummy Movie, LLC v. Mattel, Inc., 601 F.3d 1387, 1391 (Fed. Cir. 2010).

Genericness presents a related but distinct theory of abandonment. This arises when the primary significance of a term to the relevant public becomes the product or service itself rather than the source. Classic examples include “aspirin” and “escalator,” both of which lost trademark status through widespread generic use. 

Certain owner conduct relevant to abandonment based on nonuse, such as naked licensing or failure to police a mark, can also weaken the strength of a mark and contribute to its slide into generic status. Still, a mark may become generic even in the absence of owner fault. Once the public comes to understand a designation as the name of a product rather than a brand, trademark protection is lost regardless of how that shift occurred.

Unlike equitable defenses, two of which are discussed below, abandonment applies regardless of the equities between the parties. 

Laches

Laches is an equitable defense that bars relief when a plaintiff has unreasonably delayed asserting trademark rights and the delay has caused undue prejudice to the defendant. The doctrine is based on the Lanham Act’s command that remedies for infringement are subject to principles of equity. 15 U.S.C. §§ 1116(a), 1117(a).

Although phrased differently across circuits, courts generally require a defendant to show:

  1. The claimant unreasonably delayed enforcement after it knew or should have known of the challenged use; and
  2. The defendant suffered prejudice as a result of that delay.

See Kars 4 Kids Inc. v. America Can!, 8 F.4th 209, 220 (3d Cir. 2021); Elvis Presley Enters., Inc. v. Capece, 141 F.3d 188, 205 (5th Cir. 1998).

Most courts measure delay by reference to the most analogous state statute of limitations. Excelled Sheepskin & Leather Coat Corp. v. Or. Brewing Co., 897 F.3d 413, 419 (2d Cir. 2018). When delay exceeds that benchmark, a presumption of laches often arises, shifting the burden to the plaintiff to show that the delay was excusable or nonprejudicial. Id. at 420.

Prejudice typically takes two forms: economic prejudice, where the defendant has invested substantially in the mark during the period of inaction, and evidentiary prejudice, where delay results in lost witnesses or faded memories. Eat Right Foods Ltd. v. Whole Foods Mkt., Inc., 880 F.3d 1109, 1119–20 (9th Cir. 2018).

In some circumstances, courts temper laches with the doctrine of progressive encroachment, which recognizes that a trademark owner is not required to sue at the first sign of minimal use and may wait until the defendant’s conduct materially expands into its market or trade channels. See Kellogg Co. v. Exxon Corp., 209 F.3d 562, 573–74 (6th Cir. 2000); Pro Fitness Physical Therapy Ctr. v. Pro-Fit Orthopedic & Sports Physical Therapy P.C., 314 F.3d 62, 70 (2d Cir. 2002).

As to remedies, laches most often prevents a plaintiff from recovering damages. Courts are more hesitant to deny injunctive relief when confusion is likely, but in extreme cases of prejudice, some courts will deny an injunction on the basis of laches. See Tillamook Country Smoker, Inc. v. Tillamook Country Creamery Ass’n, 465 F.3d 1102, 1111 (9th Cir. 2006). 

Acquiescence

Acquiescence resembles laches, but requires active consent, not just passive delay. The defense arises when a trademark owner, through words or conduct, conveys assurance that it will not assert its rights against the defendant. To establish acquiescence, courts typically require proof of:

  1. Express or implied consent to the defendant’s use;
  2. Reliance by the defendant; and
  3. Resulting prejudice.

See Elvis Presley Enters., 141 F.3d at 206.

Although acquiescence need not be explicit, silence alone rarely suffices. Acquiescence is more often found where a trademark owner has affirmatively encouraged use, entered into cooperative arrangements without reserving rights, or failed to object in the face of clear knowledge and opportunity to do so. Because the defense rests on estoppel principles, it focuses on fairness between the parties rather than on the strength of the mark itself. 

Conclusion

Together, these common defenses protect truthful, descriptive use, require marks to remain meaningful indicators of source, and impose equitable limits when rights are asserted too late or inconsistently. Understanding them is important both for trademark owners and for parties accused of infringement.
