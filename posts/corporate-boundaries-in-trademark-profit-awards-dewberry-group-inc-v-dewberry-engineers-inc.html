Corporate Boundaries in Trademark Profit Awards: Dewberry Group, Inc. v. Dewberry Engineers, Inc.

The U.S. Supreme Court’s unanimous February 26, 2025, decision in Dewberry Group, Inc. v. Dewberry Engineers, Inc. , 604 U.S. 321 (2025) , marked a significant reaffirmation of corporate separateness in trademark infringement remedies.

Alec McNiff · 5 min read

The U.S. Supreme Court’s unanimous February 26, 2025, decision in Dewberry Group, Inc. v. Dewberry Engineers, Inc., 604 U.S. 321 (2025), marked a significant reaffirmation of corporate separateness in trademark infringement remedies. Authored by Justice Kagan, the Court held that profit disgorgement under 15 U.S.C. § 1117(a) is limited to the named defendant’s earnings, absent veil-piercing. This ruling reinforces limits on equitable relief and highlights strategic pleading for trademark plaintiffs. As of January 2026, the case remains pending on remand to the Fourth Circuit.

Case Background

The dispute stemmed from a trademark dispute between two unrelated real estate companies sharing the “Dewberry” name. Dewberry Engineers, a long-established engineering firm, holds a registered trademark in “Dewberry” for real estate services. In 2007, it sued Dewberry Group, a real estate development company owned by John Dewberry, for infringement, leading to a settlement that restricted Dewberry Group’s use of the name.

By 2020, Dewberry Group violated the agreement through a rebranding effort, creating brands like Dewberry Living, Dewberry Office, and Studio Dewberry. Dewberry Engineers filed suit in the Eastern District of Virginia, claiming Dewberry Group’s rebranding breached the agreement and infringed under the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. § 1117(a)). Dewberry Group provides services to non-party affiliates that own commercial rental properties, and earns only modest fees while the affiliates record the rental profits. This structure became central to the damages phase.

Procedural History

The Eastern District of Virginia found Dewberry Group liable for willful infringement. At a bench trial, the court awarded $43 million in disgorged profits, treating Dewberry Group and its affiliates as a “single entity” to capture the “economic reality” of their operations.

In a split decision, the Fourth Circuit affirmed, reasoning that ignoring corporate formalities prevented evasion of liability. The dissent argued disgorgement should be limited to the defendant’s profits absent veil-piercing.

Parties’ Arguments on Appeal to the Supreme Court

Dewberry Group argued that the Lanham Act’s text limits disgorgement to the “defendant’s profits,” meaning only the named defendant’s earnings—here, Dewberry Group's modest fees, not the affiliates' rental income. They contended that treating affiliates as part of the defendant ignored corporate separateness, a bedrock principle of U.S. law, absent veil-piercing (which Dewberry Engineers did not pursue). Dewberry Group emphasized that Congress did not intend for courts to routinely aggregate affiliate profits without explicit statutory authority, warning that doing so would undermine predictable business planning.

Dewberry Engineers argued that the Lanham Act’s equitable remedies allows courts to consider the “economic reality” of an infringer’s operations to prevent infringers from evading liability through affiliates. Specifically, Dewberry Engineers highlighted the “just-sum provision” (§ 1117(a)), which states that courts may exercise their discretion and adjust the award to a “just” amount if the amount of recovery based on profits is “inadequate or excessive.” Dewberry Engineers argued that this provision permits adjustments for inadequate or excessive awards, including incorporating affiliate profits to reflect the defendant’s “true financial gain.” Dewberry Engineers portrayed Dewberry Group’s structure as manipulative, with profits shifted to affiliates to shield infringement proceeds, and urged the Court to uphold the lower courts’ flexible approach to ensure the Act’s deterrent purpose.

Supreme Court Ruling

The Supreme Court vacated the award, holding that “defendant’s profits,” as used in § 1117(a) of the Lanham Act, means only the named defendant’s earnings. The Court reasoned that “defendant” carries its ordinary meaning—the sued party—and federal statutes incorporate traditional corporate law principles treating affiliates as distinct unless veil-pierced for fraud or similar abuses. Absent such a showing (which Dewberry Engineers conceded it did not make), aggregating profits violates this rule. The opinion rejected the lower courts’ “single entity” approach as unsupported by the Act’s text, which targets unjust enrichment without authorizing routine disregard of formalities. The Court stated, in no uncertain terms, that “the ‘defendant’s profits’ are the defendant’s profits, not its plus its affiliates’.” Dewberry Group, Inc. v. Dewberry Engineers Inc., 604 U.S. 321, 327 (2025).

The Court also rejected Dewberry Engineers’s reliance on the “just-sum provision,” noting that the lower courts did not invoke it. Those courts simply aggregated all of the Dewberry entities’ real-estate profits for the relevant years, rather than identifying which of the affiliates’ profits were properly attributable to Dewberry Group, and adjusting the award to an amount reflecting the Dewberry Group’s own gain. In other words, the lower courts just redefined “defendant” by treating Dewberry Group and its affiliates as a single corporate entity. In doing so, the lower courts “approved an award including non-defendants’ profits—and thus went further than the Lanham Act permits.” Dewberry Group, 604 U.S. at 329. 

The Court vacated the judgment and remanded the case for a new award proceeding. In doing so, the Court left open the question of whether courts can use the “just-sum” provision to support affiliate-based awards or scrutinize  accounting to assess “true financial gain.” The remand to the Fourth Circuit remains pending, as of January 2026.

Justice Sotomayor's Concurrence

Justice Sotomayor joined the opinion fully but concurred separately to stress that corporate separateness does not require ignoring “economic realities” or “clever accounting” in calculating the named defendant’s profits. Given Dewberry Group’s reported losses versus affiliates’ substantial infringing gains, she noted potential remand tools, like scrutinizing books, attributing below-market fees, or using the “just-sum” provision, to reflect true financial benefit without aggregating non-party profits.

Implications for Businesses and Employers

This decision provides employers and businesses with greater certainty in structuring operations through affiliates to limit liability exposure. By maintaining separate entities, companies can limit disgorgement exposure, as long as they maintain formalities and grounds for veil-piercing do not exist. For example, parent companies might use subsidiaries for high-risk activities, ensuring infringement claims target only the infringing entity’s assets.

However, the ruling does not immunize affiliates from liability if properly named as defendants. Plaintiffs may now more aggressively amend complaints or pursue veil-piercing arguments, especially where a parent company is undercapitalized or has commingled funds. 

For trademark owners, the case underscores the importance of thorough pre-suit investigation to identify all potentially liable entities. In-house counsel should consider joint liability theories early and weigh the costs of broader litigation.

Overall, the ruling reinforces equitable principles in trademark remedies while preserving corporate shields, potentially influencing how employers design group structures for IP-sensitive operations. In light of this development, businesses should consult counsel to review affiliate arrangements.
