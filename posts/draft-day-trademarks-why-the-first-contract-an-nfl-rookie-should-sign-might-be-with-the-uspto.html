Draft Day Trademarks: Why the First Contract an NFL Rookie Should Sign Might Be With the USPTO

The USPTO's first-ever booth at the NFL Draft signals a shift: federal registration is becoming standard "day one" professional planning for rookies whose personal brands now arrive in the league fully formed.

Alec McNiff · 5 min read

This April, the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) made history by setting up an educational booth at the 2026 NFL Draft in Pittsburgh — its first on-the-ground presence at a major sports event. The agency went further with a World IP Day panel titled "Authenticity: The Name of the Game," led by USPTO Director John A. Squires. The discussion covered NIL, trademark protection, counterfeit merchandise, and risks from AI deepfakes. Attendees received practical resources on brand basics.

While many top rookies arrive at Draft week with endorsement deals and social media partnerships already lined up, few have secured federal protection for their most valuable asset: their own name and personal brand. The USPTO's initiative signals it's time for that to change.

NIL has changed the game, but trademark law hasn't fully caught up

Today's NFL prospects typically enter the Draft with years of NIL activity behind them: social media sponsorships, merchandise sales, appearances, and growing fanbases. That commercial activity builds substantial goodwill in their name, nickname, logo, or signature phrases. But whether they have adequate legal protection for that goodwill is a different question entirely, and in many cases, federal trademark registration could be the answer.

What registration actually delivers

A trademark, at its core, is any word, name, symbol, or other identifier that distinguishes the source of goods or services. For an athlete, that usually means their personal name, nickname, jersey number combinations, slogans, or logos used on apparel, digital content, endorsements, or experiences.

Filing a trademark application with the USPTO is neither necessary nor sufficient to create trademark rights. Common law rights arise from actual use in commerce. But federal registration stacks several concrete advantages on top of common law rights, and those advantages are important to understand.

The most immediate is priority. Filing a federal application establishes priority as of the date of that filing. If someone else tries to register a confusingly similar mark afterward for overlapping goods or services, the earlier filing date is your first line of defense. For a player whose name becomes commercially valuable overnight on Draft night, filing early puts them in a significantly stronger position if a dispute arises later.

Another advantage is incontestability. After five years of continuous use, a registered trademark becomes incontestable, meaning that certain challenges to the registration are no longer available. That status gives the mark a kind of permanent legal weight it does not have as a newer registration, and certainly not as a common law right.

Federal registration also unlocks enforcement tools that common law rights do not. For instance, U.S. Customs and Border Protection allows trademark owners to record their registrations so customs officials can intercept counterfeit merchandise at ports of entry before it reaches consumers. Additionally, online platforms like Amazon and Etsy typically give preferential treatment to registered trademark holders for takedown requests as well.

Finally, registration provides leverage for licensing. Registration provides clear evidence of rights when negotiating deals with apparel companies, video game publishers, content platforms, or sponsors, giving licensees greater confidence and potentially higher deal value.

The filing strategy is as important as the decision to file

I wrote recently about Fernando Mendoza's trademark applications, filed before the 2026 Draft. He filed two applications covering his name, but both listed only one product category: athletic apparel. That is a better position than not filing at all, but it leaves meaningful ground unprotected. A third party who launches a "Mendoza" branded video game or podcast might fall entirely outside the scope of what he actually claimed. To be clear, an athlete can only file for categories where they have a genuine intent to offer goods or services — you cannot claim everything speculatively. But most high-profile rookies can credibly claim more than athletic apparel, and the filing strategy should reflect that. The scope of what you claim matters as much as the decision to file.

Athletes with international commercial footprints should think beyond U.S. registration, and the Madrid Protocol simplifies that process considerably by allowing a single international application to extend trademark protection into over 100 countries.

College athletes and their teams have spent years mastering NIL collectives, endorsement contracts, and revenue-sharing models. Trademark strategy now deserves equal attention as part of "day one" professional planning. Problems, such as squatters filing on a player's name after they gain fame, or unauthorized merchandise, are far more expensive and difficult to resolve after the fact.

The USPTO's presence at the Draft underscores that brand protection is becoming standard practice for the next generation of athletes, and the attorneys helping rookies navigate their first professional contracts should be part of that conversation. The players who get this right from the start will have a stronger legal foundation than the generation that came before them. The ones who wait until there's a problem will spend more to fix it.
