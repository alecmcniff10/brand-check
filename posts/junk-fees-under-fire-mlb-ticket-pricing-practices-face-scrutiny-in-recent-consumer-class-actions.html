Junk Fees Under Fire: MLB Ticket Pricing Practices Face Scrutiny in Recent Consumer Class Actions

Three recent consumer class actions against Major League Baseball clubs are testing whether mandatory add-on charges on ticket purchases amount to deceptive pricing under state consumer protection laws.

Alec McNiff · 4 min read

Three recent consumer class actions against Major League Baseball clubs are testing whether mandatory add-on charges on ticket purchases amount to deceptive pricing under state consumer protection laws. Filed between September 2025 and January 2026 against the Washington Nationals, Boston Red Sox, and San Francisco Giants, the complaints share a common theory: that tickets were marketed at base prices that excluded mandatory fees disclosed only at or near checkout.

The Lawsuits

In Gustafson v. Washington Nationals Baseball Club, LLC, No. 1:25-cv-03033 (D.D.C. filed Sept. 5, 2025), the plaintiff alleges that tickets purchased at Nationals Park in May 2023 were advertised at a base price but increased by roughly 60 percent after adding service charges, handling and convenience charges, and processing fees. According to the complaint those fees surfaced only after the transaction was completed or on credit card statements received after purchase. The suit proceeds under the D.C. Consumer Protection Procedures Act and seeks nationwide class relief dating back to 2022.

In Campagna v. Boston Red Sox Baseball Club, L.P., No. 1:26-cv-10182 (D. Mass. filed Jan. 16, 2026), three plaintiffs challenge both variable per-ticket fees and flat order fees allegedly revealed only at the final checkout screen. The complaint also alleges that countdown timers and layered terms and conditions pressured consumers to complete purchases before evaluating the total cost. The action proceeds under Massachusetts and Rhode Island consumer protection statutes and proposes a nationwide class dating to early 2022.

Most recently, in Flores v. San Francisco Baseball Associates LLC, No. 3:26-cv-00849 (N.D. Cal. filed Jan. 26, 2026), the plaintiff alleges that two $10 tickets to a Giants-Padres game in April 2024 ballooned to $29 after convenience and processing fees were added at the final purchase screen. The complaint asserts that this pricing structure continued until around July 1, 2024, when California’s Honest Pricing Act (SB 478) took effect. The plaintiff asserts claims under that statute as well as California’s Consumers Legal Remedies Act, Unfair Competition Law, and False Advertising Law.

The FTC Rule as Regulatory Backdrop

These lawsuits arrive just as the FTC’s Trade Regulation Rule on Unfair or Deceptive Fees (the “Junk Fees Rule”), codified at 16 C.F.R. Part 464, took effect. Published in the Federal Register on January 10, 2025 and effective May 12, 2025, the Rule makes it an unfair or deceptive practice to advertise a price for live-event tickets without clearly and conspicuously disclosing the total price, defined as the maximum amount a consumer must pay, including all mandatory fees. The total price must appear more prominently than other pricing information and must be disclosed before the consumer consents to pay.

The types of charges at issue in these MLB cases fall squarely within the Rule’s scope. The Rule does not create a private right of action and is not directly at issue in any of the three suits, which are grounded entirely in state consumer protection statutes. Nevertheless, it reinforces the plaintiffs’ central theory by establishing at the federal level the principle that an advertised ticket price should reflect the full mandatory cost.

Why These Cases Are Worth Watching

The legal theory here is not new. Whether excluding mandatory fees from an advertised price is materially misleading, and whether consumers relied on that representation, are familiar questions under state consumer protection law. What makes these cases worth watching is the broader context they fit into.

First, the regulatory environment has shifted underneath the live-event industry in a short period of time. California’s Honest Pricing Act took effect in July 2024. The FTC’s Junk Fees Rule followed in May 2025. Together, they represent a clear trend toward requiring all-in pricing for live-event tickets, and they give plaintiffs’ lawyers a federal regulatory framework to point to even in cases brought under state law. Whether courts treat the FTC Rule’s standards as informative on questions of materiality and deception in these state-law cases will be one thread to follow.

Second, all three complaints propose nationwide classes, which means they will raise choice-of-law and class manageability issues across state statutory schemes that vary considerably in their treatment of deceptive pricing. How courts handle those issues could shape the viability of future nationwide fee-transparency class actions beyond baseball and ticketing.

Third, these cases are part of a larger wave of scrutiny hitting the live-event ticketing industry from multiple directions. In March 2026, Live Nation Entertainment settled a long-running antitrust case with the Department of Justice, agreeing to cap service fees at Ticketmaster-operated amphitheaters at 15% of the ticket price and to open its platform to competing ticket sellers. That settlement was the product of years of public pressure that intensified after the Ticketmaster platform crashed during presales for Taylor Swift’s Eras Tour in late 2022. Between that settlement, the FTC Rule, state legislation like SB 478, and now private class actions targeting individual franchises, live-event ticket pricing is facing pressure from regulators, enforcers, and consumers simultaneously.

For sports franchises and other live-event sellers, the message is straightforward: pricing practices that separate mandatory fees from advertised ticket prices now carry risk from private litigation and federal regulatory enforcement at the same time. How courts handle these early MLB cases could set the tone for what follows.
