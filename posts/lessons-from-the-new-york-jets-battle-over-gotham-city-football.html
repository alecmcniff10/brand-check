Lessons From the New York Jets’ Battle Over “Gotham City Football”

The New York Jets’ recent adoption of the phrase “Gotham City Football” through the NFL’s “Rivalries” alternate uniform program has triggered a familiar trademark problem: when does a branding phrase tied to a location become protectable?

Alec McNiff · 7 min read

The New York Jets’ recent adoption of the phrase “Gotham City Football” through the NFL’s “Rivalries” alternate uniform program has triggered a familiar trademark problem: when does a branding phrase tied to a location become protectable?

In June 2025, the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) issued a final refusal of the Jets’ trademark application. The USPTO grounded its decision in two bedrock principles of trademark law: (1) geographic descriptiveness under Section 2(e)(2) of the Lanham Act, and (2) likelihood of confusion under Section 2(d). The case offers a clear illustration of the limits of trademark protection for location-inspired branding, even when layered with fictional themes.

Unlike almost every defense in the NFL this season, the USPTO’s final refusal did not stop the Jets from moving forward. They debuted their dark, stylized “Gotham City Football” uniforms on December 7, 2025, against the Miami Dolphins. Nevertheless, the unresolved refusal is a reminder that cultural relevance alone is not enough to clear the legal hurdles to trademark registration.

Background: From Locker Room Slogan to Alternate Uniform

According to reports, the Jets have used “Gotham City Football,” and close variants, informally for years on everything from sideline apparel to locker room nameplates, and merchandise dating back to at least the mid-2010s. The phrase draws on New York City’s long-standing nickname “Gotham,” intending to frame the team’s identity around toughness and urban grit.

In 2025, that internal branding became an official alternate uniform as part of the NFL’s Nike-backed “Rivalries” program. The Jets’ design leaned heavily into the theme: matte black helmets, muted “Gotham Green” accents, Gothic-style numbering, and “Gotham City Football” printed inside collars and on helmet bumpers. The team later released apparel and merchandise, which generated significant fan interest.

To protect this expanded commercial use, the NFL filed a trademark application on the Jets’ behalf for “Gotham City Football” in International Class 25, covering apparel such as shirts, hoodies, and caps. The goal was to secure federal registration, nationwide priority, and the legal presumptions that come with it.

The USPTO’s Final Refusal

On June 12, 2025, a USPTO examining attorney issued a final office action refusing registration of the “Gotham City Football” mark on two primary grounds.

First, the USPTO deemed the mark to be primarily geographically descriptive under 15 U.S.C. § 1052(e)(2). Trademark law prohibits registration of marks that primarily describe a geographic location, as such terms should remain free for all to use in describing goods or services originating from (or associated with) that place. See, e.g., In re Nantucket, Inc., 677 F.2d 95 (C.C.P.A. 1982). The examiner concluded that “Gotham City” is widely recognized as a nickname for New York City (a real, well-known place) notwithstanding its fictional association with DC Comics’ Batman universe.

The USPTO cited historical references showing the term’s use predated Batman by over a century. For example, writer Washington Irving first popularized “Gotham” as a satirical nickname for New York City in his 1807 literary magazine Salmagundi, drawing from the English village of Gotham. This predates Batman’s debut in 1939 by over a century, undermining the Jets’ argument that consumers primarily associate “Gotham City” with the fictional crime-ridden city in the Dark Knight.

The Jets attempted to rebut this evidence by emphasizing the Batman association and arguing that consumers primarily associate “Gotham City” with the fictional setting rather than New York City. The examiner rejected that argument, finding the real-world geographic meaning dominant. Even when a term has acquired secondary meaning, a primarily geographic mark is registrable only if its primary significance is no longer geographic. See, e.g., In re Cookie Kitchen, Inc., 228 USPQ 873, 874 (TTAB 1986); Trademark Manual of Examining Procedure (TMEP) § 1210.01(a). This demanding standard is rarely met for long-established nicknames of places.

Second, the refusal cited a likelihood of confusion with an existing registration held by the “Gotham City Crew,” a popular independent New York Jets fan club established in 2015. The fan group has its own trademark for apparel and fan services, and the examiner determined that consumers might mistakenly believe “Gotham City Football” merchandise originates from or is affiliated with the Crew. Factors like similar goods (apparel), shared channels of trade (sports merchandise), and overlapping consumers (Jets fans) weighed heavily in this analysis.

Practical Implications: Registration Denied, But Use Continues

In August 2025, the Jets requested and received a three-month extension, pushing their response deadline to December 12, 2025. As of early 2026, no public update indicates whether the Jets filed  an appeal or abandoned the application. An appeal could proceed to the Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB), with the potential for further review in federal court.

Importantly, refusal does not bar use. Federal registration provides valuable benefits, including nationwide constructive notice, enhanced remedies in infringement suits, and incontestability after five years of use, but unregistered marks can still be protected under Section 43(a) of the Lanham Act or common law. The Jets rely on these, plus licensing agreements with merchandise partners, to police unauthorized use. Historical precedents, like the Washington Commanders (formerly Redskins) continuing brand enforcement despite past registration challenges, demonstrate that teams can still thrive without registration for every slogan.

Notably absent from the dispute is any public opposition from DC Comics or Warner Bros., which hold trademarks for “Gotham City” in entertainment contexts (e.g., films, comics). Past tensions centered on the Gotham City Crew’s logo, which DC Comics opposed in 2018. The Jets’ purely textual use of “Gotham City” has not drawn similar scrutiny.

Lessons for Trademark Practitioners and Business Owners

The Jets’ trademark dispute offers practical guidance for potential registrants. Below are five key takeaways.

  1. Geographic Marks Are Difficult to Register

Even with fictional overlays, if evidence shows primary public association with a real place, refusal is likely. Applicants could benefit from conducting thorough consumer surveys early to build evidence of non-geographic significance.

  1. Conduct Thorough Searches and Anticipate Overlaps

The Jets’ refusal partly stemmed from a likelihood of confusion under Section 2(d) with the “Gotham City Crew,” an independent Jets fan club that holds a federal registration for similar apparel and fan services. Because the parties targeted the same audience (Jets fans) through overlapping goods and trade channels, the USPTO found confusion likely, showing that even supportive fan communities can inadvertently block official team marks. Applicants should conduct thorough prior art searches beyond standard USPTO records (including state registrations, social media, e-commerce platforms, and fan club websites) and secure consent agreements or assignments to reduce potential conflicts. This is especially true in fan-driven industries like sports and entertainment. 

  1. Evaluate Alternative Options for Protection

For descriptive or geographic terms, applicants should consider protecting stylized logos, which are often registrable even when the wording alone is weak. State registrations and common-law rights can also provide meaningful, albeit narrower, protection. In some cases, the Supplemental Register can offer an interim path if a mark is capable of acquiring distinctiveness but has not yet done so.

  1. File Early and Plan for Examination Delays 

By filing an intent-to-use application ahead of the 2025 uniform launch, the Jets secured priority and were able to roll out merchandise despite later office actions and a final refusal. Still, the prolonged examination underscores the risk that geographic or descriptive issues can derail timelines during high-visibility launches. Best practice is to file ITU applications well before public announcements, prepare rebuttal evidence (e.g., secondary meaning data) early, and pursue parallel stylized or design marks. That approach preserves filing-date priority and allows launches to proceed while continuing to work through refusals or appeals.

  1. Build a Record for Acquired Distinctiveness From the Outset

The USPTO based its refusal in part on primary geographic descriptiveness under Section 2(e)(2), insisting that “Gotham City” is viewed primarily as New York City’s historic nickname rather than the Jets’ brand, despite years of informal use. Registration can still be achieved under Section 2(f) by proving acquired distinctiveness through substantial evidence of exclusive use, such as sales figures, advertising spend, media coverage, and consumer surveys demonstrating source identification. Clients using place-based or descriptive branding should systematically collect this evidence from initial adoption, as it often determines whether a refusal can be overcome.

Conclusion

The Jets’ “Gotham City Football” saga highlights the tension in trademark law between exclusive rights and public access to descriptive terms. For sports franchises closely linked to local identity, overcoming geographic refusals can be a challenge. The Jets’ continued use and successful marketing of the brand without federal registration proves that strong common-law rights and fan loyalty can carry significant weight, though federal registration still offers the clearest and most reliable protection.
