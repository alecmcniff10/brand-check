Likelihood of Confusion Isn’t One Test: Why Circuit Differences in Trademark Law Actually Matter

A key element of any trademark infringement claim is the likelihood of confusion. A plaintiff must show that the defendant’s use of a mark is likely to confuse ordinary consumers as to the source, sponsorship, affiliation, or approval of the goods or services.

Alec McNiff · 4 min read

A key element of any trademark infringement claim is the likelihood of confusion. A plaintiff must show that the defendant’s use of a mark is likely to confuse ordinary consumers as to the source, sponsorship, affiliation, or approval of the goods or services. Although federal courts generally agree on this ultimate inquiry, they apply different multi-factor tests depending on the circuit. These differences are often described as minor or semantic, but in practice they can meaningfully affect outcomes, litigation strategy, and forum selection.

This article highlights key distinctions among the likelihood-of-confusion tests applied across the federal courts, all of which treat their factors as non-exhaustive and context-dependent.

The Polaroid and Sleekcraft Models: Similar Inputs, Different Emphases

The Second Circuit’s Polaroid test, articulated in Polaroid Corporation v. Polarad Electronics Corporation, 287 F.2d 492 (2d Cir. 1961), and the Ninth Circuit’s Sleekcraft test, from AMF Incorporated v. Sleekcraft Boats, 599 F.2d 341 (9th Cir. 1979), are the most representative frameworks for understanding likelihood-of-confusion analysis because nearly every other circuit’s formulation maps onto one of their two basic approaches. The tests are strikingly similar with respect to their relevant factors:

Polaroid factors (Second Circuit)

  1. Strength of the plaintiff’s mark
  2. Similarity of the marks
  3. Proximity of the products or services
  4. Likelihood that the prior owner will bridge the gap
  5. Evidence of actual confusion
  6. Defendant’s good or bad faith (intent)
  7. Quality of the defendant’s product or service
  8. Sophistication of the relevant consumers

Sleekcraft factors (Ninth Circuit)

  1. Strength of the mark
  2. Proximity or relatedness of the goods or services
  3. Similarity of the marks
  4. Evidence of actual confusion
  5. Marketing channels used
  6. Type of goods and the degree of care likely to be exercised by purchasers
  7. Defendant’s intent in selecting the mark
  8. Likelihood of expansion of the product lines

As indicated by the bold font, both tests examine mark strength, similarity, proximity of the goods or services, evidence of actual confusion, the defendant’s intent, and consumer care. But despite this overlap, the tests guide courts toward different kinds of evidence and analytical emphases.

Sleekcraft focuses on how consumers actually encounter marks in the marketplace. By separating marketing channels, degree of consumer care, and type of goods into distinct factors, it requires courts to analyze how confusion would occur in practice. The test maps onto the consumer’s path through the marketplace and encourages evidence about advertising platforms, sales environments, pricing, and purchasing behavior.

Polaroid, on the other hand, is more conceptual and flexible. It collapses those same components into broader questions about “proximity” and “consumer sophistication,” leaving courts more room to emphasize or downplay particular marketplace details as they see fit. As such, a court applying the Polaroid test may or may not evaluate marketing channels as part of its “proximity” or “consumer sophistication” factors, whereas a court bound by Sleekcraft would do so expressly. As a result, two courts can apply these “substantially similar” tests and still reach different results on the same facts.

The DuPont Factors: A Different Tool for a Different Job

The Federal Circuit and the Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB) apply a more comprehensive list of up to thirteen factors, known as the DuPont factors. Unlike Polaroid and Sleekcraft, DuPont expressly accounts for the broader market structure, by including factors such as the number of similar marks in use, length of use, coexistence without confusion, and any other factors probative of confusion.

This breadth makes sense in the registration context, where the Patent and Trademark Office (PTO) must consider not just the parties before it, but the integrity of the register as a whole. The drawback of the DuPont test, however, is its tendency to place less emphasis on real-world consumer perception between the relevant products, which is the core concern in infringement litigation. 

Tests in Other Circuits

The remaining circuits apply likelihood-of-confusion tests that track the same core considerations, but with distinct formulations and emphases.

  • The First Circuit applies the Pignons factors, focusing heavily on similarity, channels of trade, and evidence of actual confusion.
  • The Third Circuit’s Lapp factors are more detailed and resemble Sleekcraft in their attention to market conditions and purchaser care.
  • The Fourth Circuit uses the Pizzeria Uno factors, which tend to emphasize mark strength and similarity.
  • The Fifth Circuit’s “Digits of Confusion” test closely parallels Polaroid and often gives substantial weight to intent and actual confusion.
  • The Sixth Circuit applies the Frisch factors, which are structurally similar to Sleekcraft but often applied more holistically.
  • The Seventh Circuit favors a flexible, evidence-driven approach that downplays formal factor counting and emphasizes consumer perception.
  • The Eighth Circuit’s SquirtCo factors resemble Polaroid but frequently stress competitive proximity.
  • The Tenth Circuit applies six factors, sometimes called the “Beer Nuts factors,” which place particular emphasis on similarity and market overlap.
  • The Eleventh Circuit’s Frehling factors closely track Polaroid and Sleekcraft but often elevate mark strength and similarity.

These differences rarely change the legal standard, but they influence how courts weigh evidence.

Practical Implications

For litigants, these differences affect everything from forum selection to discovery priorities and evidentiary strategy. In a Sleekcraft jurisdiction, for example, marketing channels and purchasing context deserve early attention, whereas in courts applying Polaroid, strength and similarity evidence may dominate. In TTAB proceedings, failure to address DuPont-specific market structure factors can be fatal.

The broader takeaway is that likelihood of confusion is not a single test with local labels. It is a family of related frameworks that reward different kinds of evidence and different litigation strategies. Litigators should identify these circuit-specific differences at the outset and tailor their evidence and arguments to the governing framework.
