Understanding the laws that protect brands.

Major Record Labels Sue Quince for Using Copyrighted Music in Social Media Marketing

A group of major record labels and music publishers, including UMG Recordings, Capitol Records, and Concord Music Group, filed a federal copyright infringement lawsuit against Quince on April 16 in the Northern District of California. The complaint alleges that Quince used copyrighted music without authorization in hundreds of social media videos promoting its products on TikTok and Instagram, and that it continued doing so even after being put on notice more than a year ago.

The case is UMG Recordings, Inc. et al. v. Last Brand, Inc. d/b/a Quince, No. 3:26-cv-03244 (N.D. Cal.).

Quince is a direct-to-consumer fashion startup founded in 2018 that has expanded into luggage, bedding, and other product categories. The company has built its brand almost entirely through social media, working with roughly 300 influencers per month to promote its products. That strategy has apparently been effective: Quince recently raised $500 million at a reported $10.1 billion valuation. But according to the complaint, the company’s social media engine has been running on unlicensed music, and the plaintiffs have catalogued 67 sound recordings and 71 musical compositions that were allegedly used without permission.

The complaint identifies specific posts on Quince’s own accounts featuring songs by Sabrina Carpenter, Fleetwood Mac, Britney Spears, Etta James, and Brenton Wood, among others. But the plaintiffs go further than targeting Quince’s own posts. They allege that Quince directed, commissioned, reviewed, and redistributed influencer content that also contained unlicensed music, and that the company encouraged influencers to use “trending audio” (which often includes copyrighted songs) to boost engagement and trigger platform algorithms that amplify reach.

One detail stands out: the complaint alleges that Quince reposted an influencer’s video on its own TikTok account and made a single change, swapping the original audio for a copyrighted song owned by one of the plaintiffs. That kind of affirmative act undercuts any argument that the music use was incidental or the influencer’s choice alone.

The plaintiffs sent Quince a demand letter in September 2024 identifying infringing posts. After more than a month of silence and a follow-up, Quince responded through counsel in November 2024, claiming it had “fully addressed” the copyright concerns. The complaint alleges that was false. Plaintiffs later found that pre-existing infringing posts were still live and that new ones had been posted, including at least seven in March 2026 alone.

Why this case matters beyond Quince

The lawsuit brings four counts: direct copyright infringement by the record label plaintiffs, direct infringement by the music publisher plaintiffs, contributory infringement, and vicarious infringement.

The direct infringement claims are relatively straightforward and follow a pattern that has already been tested in court. In 2022, a federal court in Florida granted partial summary judgment to Sony Music against Bang Energy (the energy drink brand) on nearly identical facts, finding that Bang had directly posted approximately 286 social media videos containing copyrighted music without authorization. That ruling, Sony Music Entm’t v. Vital Pharmaceuticals, Inc., No. 21-22825-CIV (S.D. Fla. Sept. 14, 2022), is cited repeatedly in the Quince complaint. There is also a companion case, UMG Recs., Inc. v. Vital Pharms., Inc., No. 21-CV-60914-CIV (S.D. Fla. July 11, 2022), in which UMG similarly prevailed on summary judgment.

I think the direct infringement claims against Quince for posts on its own accounts are strong. The law here is clear: both TikTok and Instagram’s terms of service explicitly state that music available on the platform is licensed only for personal, noncommercial use. Using copyrighted music in branded promotional content without a separate license (typically a synchronization license for the composition and a master use license for the recording) is infringement. Quince is a sophisticated company, with a head of brand who previously worked at Spotify, and it was put on notice over a year before the complaint was filed. The willfulness allegations are not incidental, as statutory damages for willful infringement can reach $150,000 per work.

The more interesting legal question involves the contributory and vicarious infringement claims tied to influencer content. The plaintiffs allege that Quince provided creative briefs to influencers, reviewed and approved their content, encouraged them to follow social media trends (including trending audio), compensated them, and reposted their videos. If those allegations hold up, they could establish that Quince had both the knowledge and the material contribution necessary for contributory liability, as well as the right and ability to supervise, along with a direct financial benefit, necessary for vicarious liability.

The Supreme Court’s recent decision in Cox Communications, Inc. v. Sony Music Entertainment, No. 24-171 (Mar. 25, 2026), clarified just how narrow contributory copyright liability is: a provider is liable only if it induced the infringement or provided a service tailored to infringement. Knowledge alone, even extensive knowledge, is not enough. But that framework actually maps well onto the allegations against Quince. This is not a case about a general-purpose service provider that passively enabled infringement. The complaint describes a company that directed influencers to create specific content, provided them with creative briefs, encouraged the use of unlicensed trending audio to boost engagement, reviewed and approved the resulting videos, and then reposted them on its own channels. That is inducement, and it falls squarely within the category of conduct that Cox confirmed will still support contributory liability.

This matters because the Quince case is part of a broader enforcement wave by music companies targeting brands that use unlicensed music in social media advertising. Similar suits have been filed against Gymshark, Marriott Hotels, the University of Southern California, DSW, Crumbl, PacSun, and Chili’s parent company Brinker International, among others. Many of those cases have settled. But the secondary liability theories in this complaint, the idea that a brand can be held liable not just for its own posts but for influencer content it directs, funds, and promotes, have the potential to reshape how brands structure their influencer relationships.

For any brand running a social media marketing program that relies on influencers and creator partnerships, this case is a clear signal. If you are directing influencers to use trending audio, providing creative briefs, reviewing their content before it goes live, and reposting it on your own channels, you may be exposed to contributory and vicarious copyright liability for any unlicensed music that appears in that content. Obtaining proper music licenses, or requiring influencers to use only royalty-free or properly licensed audio, is a basic compliance step that can prevent exactly this kind of litigation.
