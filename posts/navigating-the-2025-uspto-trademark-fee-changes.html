Navigating the 2025 USPTO Trademark Fee Changes

The United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) introduced major adjustments to trademark fees on January 18, 2025, with some Madrid Protocol-related changes effective February 18, 2025.

Alec McNiff · 3 min read

The United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) introduced major adjustments to trademark fees on January 18, 2025, with some Madrid Protocol-related changes effective February 18, 2025. These updates mark the first significant revision in several years and reflect the agency’s efforts to recover rising operational costs, incentivize efficient filing practices, and fund initiatives under its 2022–2026 Strategic Plan. 

Key Fee Changes

The changes eliminate the previous two-tiered filing system—TEAS Plus at $250 per class and TEAS Standard at $350 per class. Replacing it is a unified base application fee of $350 per class for applications under Sections 1 (use-based or intent-to-use) and 44 (foreign-based) of the Trademark Act. Although this simplifies the process by removing strict TEAS Plus requirements, it raises the entry cost for many applicants who previously qualified for the lower tier.

To encourage completeness and standardization, the USPTO introduced new surcharges. A $100 per-class fee now applies if applications lack essential information, such as the applicant’s name, domicile address, entity type, translations for non-English terms, or detailed descriptions for design marks. More significantly, applicants must use pre-approved identifications of goods and services from the USPTO’s Trademark ID Manual to avoid extra costs. Opting for custom free-form text incurs a $200 per-class surcharge for the first 1,000 characters, with an additional $200 for each subsequent 1,000 characters. These length-based fees are intended to discourage overly verbose descriptions that extend examination time.

Post-registration fees also rose to offset increased processing demands, particularly for reviewing specimens of use. Declarations under Section 8 (affirming continued use, required between years 5–6 and with renewals) jumped from $225 to $325 per class. Section 9 renewal applications (required every 10 years) increased from $300 to $325 per class, while Section 15 declarations of incontestability rose from $200 to $250 per class. Fees for amendments to allege use or statements of use now cost $150 per class, up from $100, reflecting higher scrutiny of “questionable” specimens.

Other notable hikes include petitions to the Director (from $250 to $400), petitions to revive abandoned applications (from $150 to $250), and letters of protest by third parties (from $50 to $150).

Impacts on Trademark Filings 

These adjustments carry broad implications for trademark strategy. The USPTO anticipates that most filers will pay only the $350 base fee by submitting complete applications with ID Manual descriptions, potentially speeding up examinations and benefiting the system overall. However, applicants with complex or unique goods/services, which are common in tech, fashion, or software sectors, may see costs soar, as surcharges can add hundreds or thousands per application in multi-class cases.

Small businesses, startups, and individual filers could feel disproportionate impacts, as the loss of the $250 option and new penalties for customization raise barriers to entry. In addition, large portfolio owners will also be affected due to maintenance costs that are likely to increase. As a result, they will more carefully review unused registrations to avoid unnecessary renewals.

On the upside, the new structure incentivizes best practices. Thorough preparation should reduce office actions, and standardized filings could shorten waiting times. The changes also aim to deter low-quality or fraudulent applications that clog the register.

Strategies to Navigate Fee Changes

Penalties can often be avoided by relying heavily on the Trademark ID Manual for goods and services descriptions and limiting free-form language to situations where it is genuinely necessary. Applicants should also edit aggressively to remain within the 1,000-character limit per class and ensure all required applicant information is collected upfront to avoid surcharges. For intent-to-use applications, confirming a realistic path to commercialization before filing is now increasingly important. Regular portfolio audits can further reduce costs by identifying registrations that no longer justify ongoing maintenance.

Consulting experienced trademark counsel remains advisable, especially for complex identifications or international filings, as professional guidance can prevent costly surcharges and office actions.

Conclusion

The 2025 fee revisions balance fiscal sustainability with incentives for quality, aiming for a more reliable and efficient U.S. trademark ecosystem. While costs rise in targeted areas, proactive compliance can keep expenses manageable and yield faster, stronger protections. Always check the official USPTO fee schedule for the latest details.
