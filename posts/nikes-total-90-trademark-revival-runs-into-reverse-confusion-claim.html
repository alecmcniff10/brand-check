Nike’s Total 90 Trademark Revival Runs Into Reverse Confusion Claim

Nike is defending its revived Total 90 soccer line against trademark infringement claims brought by a smaller company that obtained federal registration for the mark after Nike’s earlier registration lapsed.

Alec McNiff · 6 min read

Nike is defending its revived Total 90 soccer line against trademark infringement claims brought by a smaller company that obtained federal registration for the mark after Nike’s earlier registration lapsed. In Total90 LLC v. Nike, Inc., No. 2:25-cv-02325 (E.D. La.), filed in November 2025, the dispute centers on whether Nike’s asserted common-law rights and continued use prevail over the plaintiff’s registration. The court denied the plaintiff’s motion for temporary restraining order (TRO), but the case underscores the risks of letting legacy marks lapse, especially before major marketing campaigns.

Case Background

Nike launched its iconic Total 90 soccer cleat line in 2000 but allowed its federal registration for “TOTAL 90” to lapse in 2019 as primary use declined. In 2022, Hugh Bartlett, a New Orleans–based engineer and youth soccer coach, obtained a federal registration for the nearly identical mark “TOTAL90” through Total90 LLC, based on use beginning in 2019 for a fantasy soccer app and later expanding into apparel and footwear.

Nike began relaunching its retro Total 90 products in March 2025, seeking to capitalize on nostalgia ahead of the 2026 World Cup. In December 2024, Bartlett allegedly contacted Nike to propose a collaboration. Negotiations continued for nearly a year but soured when, according to Nike, Bartlett demanded $2.5 million to assign the registration or face litigation. Total90 LLC then filed suit in November 2025, asserting claims for trademark infringement, unfair competition, and reverse confusion under the Lanham Act. Total90 LLC sought a TRO, preliminary injunction, damages, and destruction of infringing materials.

Total90 LLC argued that Nike abandoned the “TOTAL 90” mark when its federal registration lapsed in 2019, and that Nike’s high-profile relaunch was creating reverse confusion, causing consumers to mistakenly associate Total90’s products with Nike. Nike countered by asserting its senior-user rights, emphasizing continuous use of the mark since 2000. While acknowledging the expired registration, Nike maintained that it had never discontinued use or forfeited its significant brand equity in the mark. On November 26, 2025, Chief Judge Wendy B. Vitter denied Total90’s motion for a temporary restraining order, finding insufficient evidence of likelihood of success or irreparable harm. A preliminary injunction hearing is scheduled for January 8, 2026. 

Key Takeaways for Trademark Practitioners and Brand Owners

Although the case is still pending, it already highlights key principles regarding abandonment, priority, and the limits of federal registration.

1. Abandonment Is Not Automatic Upon Lapsed Registration

A core issue in the case is whether Nike abandoned its rights to the “TOTAL 90”mark by failing to renew. Under 15 U.S.C. § 1127, a mark is abandoned when “its use has been discontinued with intent not to resume,” or when the owner “causes the mark to become the generic name for the goods or services on or in connection with which it is used or otherwise to lose its significance as a mark.” Nonuse for three consecutive years creates a rebuttable presumption of abandonment, but the burden is on the challenger to prove both discontinuance and intent. Mere failure to renew a registration does not constitute abandonment. Jean Patou, Inc. v. Aristocrat Products Corp., 202 U.S.P.Q. 130, 1979 WL 24833 (T.T.A.B. 1979). And courts routinely recognize that the lapse of a federal registration does not extinguish common-law rights where bona fide use continues. See, e.g., Crash Dummy Movie, LLC v. Mattel, Inc., 601 F.3d 1387, 1391 (Fed. Cir. 2010). As Judge Vitter noted, U.S. trademark rights flow from use, not registration. 

Here, Nike offered evidence of continued commercial use after its registration lapsed, including resale activity, limited releases, and existing inventory, which undermined any claim of abandonment. The takeaway for brand owners is that even minimal ongoing use of dormant marks should be documented to defend against abandonment claims. At the same time, while residual or secondary-market sales can preserve priority for dormant product lines, extended minimal use increases vulnerability, especially if a third party later adopts and registers the mark in good faith.

2. Senior Common-Law Rights Trump Junior Registration

Priority for marks is determined by first use in commerce rather than registration. This means a senior common-law user’s prior rights prevail over a junior party’s later federal registration, even if incontestable. The registration’s presumptions of exclusivity under § 1057(b) and § 1115(a) are rebuttable by evidence of earlier use (§ 1115(b)(5)), allowing the prior user to continue using the mark in geographic regions in which it used the mark prior to the junior party’s registration. Courts have consistently upheld this principle. See Wrist-Rocket Mfg. Co. v. Saunders Archery Co., 578 F.2d 727, 731 (8th Cir. 1978) (incontestability yields to prior common-law rights); Old Dutch Foods, Inc. v. Dan Dee Pretzel & Potato Chip Co., 477 F.2d 150, 157 (6th Cir. 1973); Burger King of Florida, Inc. v. Hoots, 403 F.2d 904, 909 (7th Cir. 1968). 

In Nike v. Total90 LLC, the court’s denial of a TRO against Nike reflects this framework, signaling that Nike’s decades of prior use (since 2000) likely establish senior common-law rights that override Total90 LLC’s 2022 registration, permitting Nike’s continued sales pending full adjudication.

3. Challenges in Proving Reverse Confusion

Total90 LLC advances a reverse confusion theory, alleging that Nike’s massive promotional efforts and market dominance in relaunching “Total 90” have overwhelmed its smaller brand, causing consumers to mistakenly believe Total90 LLC’s goods are affiliated with, or sponsored by, Nike, eroding the junior registrant’s distinct identity. Although reverse confusion is a recognized doctrine (e.g., Dreamwerks Prod. Grp., Inc. v. SKG Studio, 142 F.3d 1127, 1130 (9th Cir. 1998), it demands robust proof of likelihood of confusion, including actual evidence of consumer misassociation. In denying emergency relief, the court found Total90 LLC’s preliminary showing lacking, noting the absence of evidence of actual confusion and the use of distinct retail channels. The court also emphasized Total90 LLC’s 11-month delay in seeking a TRO after negotiations, which undercut any claim of irreparable harm under the Trademark Modernization Act.

This aspect of the dispute highlights the high evidentiary bar for reverse confusion claims, particularly where a smaller junior registrant faces a senior common-law user with substantial legacy goodwill. Success typically requires early, persuasive evidence of actual consumer confusion, such as surveys, misdirected inquiries, or anecdotal proof, coupled with prompt action to preserve any presumption of irreparable harm. Brand owners who acquire lapsed marks should begin gathering confusion evidence as soon as a legacy brand reenters the market, closely monitor overlapping channels, and avoid extended negotiations that may undermine claims of urgency. Conversely, larger brands reviving dormant marks benefit from documenting continuous, even limited, use to defeat abandonment arguments.

4. Portfolio Management and Revival Risks

Nike’s experience offers a practical lesson for brands reviving legacy marks. Even well-known names benefit from consistent portfolio maintenance, including timely § 8 and § 9 filings and routine monitoring for third-party registrations. Docketing systems and periodic audits of dormant marks help reduce the risk that nostalgic brands are picked up by opportunistic registrants.

For smaller companies, acquiring a lapsed registration can create leverage, but enforcing it against a senior user with substantial historical goodwill carries real risk, including challenges based on prior use or allegations of bad-faith adoption. As the case moves forward, discovery may shed additional light on Nike’s post-2019 use and intent, and a decision on the merits could clarify how courts distinguish token use from bona fide commercial use in brand-revival scenarios.

Conclusion

In sum, although Nike cleared the initial TRO hurdle, the dispute underscores an important principle: trademark rights are grounded in use, not registration alone. For brand owners, the case reinforces the importance of disciplined portfolio maintenance, maintaining evidence of ongoing use, and acting promptly when revived marks attract competing third-party claims.
