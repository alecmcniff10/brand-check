Ninth Circuit Affirms Dismissal in Hara v. Netflix: Balancing Trademarks and Artistic Freedom

The Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals recently affirmed the dismissal of a Lanham Act lawsuit brought by drag performer Vicky Vox against Netflix and related parties.

Alec McNiff · 4 min read

The Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals recently affirmed the dismissal of a Lanham Act lawsuit brought by drag performer Vicky Vox against Netflix and related parties. In Hara v. Netflix, Inc. (No. 23-3768, decided July 28, 2025), the court clarified the application of key legal tests in trademark disputes involving expressive media. The decision provides important guidance for those in creative fields like film or television, as it offers insights into navigating the boundary between brand protection and artistic expression in the wake of the Supreme Court’s decision in Jack Daniel’s Properties, Inc. v. VIP Products LLC.

The Legal Framework: Rogers, Jack Daniel’s, and Source Identification

The Lanham Act protects against consumer confusion regarding the origin, affiliation, or endorsement of goods or services. For celebrities, that protection can extend to unauthorized uses of their name, image, or likeness that imply sponsorship, which are typically analyzed under the likelihood-of-confusion test. See Twentieth Century Fox Television v. Empire Distrib., Inc., 875 F.3d 1192, 1196 (9th Cir. 2017).

When the challenged use appears in an expressive work, however, the Ninth Circuit applies the test articulated in Rogers v. Grimaldi, 875 F.2d 994 (2d Cir. 1989). Under Rogers, the Lanham Act does not apply unless the use of the mark has no artistic relevance to the underlying work or explicitly misleads consumers as to the source or origin of the work.

The continued viability of Rogers was called into question by the Supreme Court’s 2023 decision in Jack Daniel’s. There, the Court held that Rogers does not apply when a defendant uses a mark as a source identifier for its own goods, as was the case with a parodic dog toy designed to mimic a Jack Daniel’s whiskey bottle. In cases decided after Jack Daniel’s, including Punchbowl, Inc. v. AJ Press, LLC, 90 F.4th 1022 (9th Cir. 2024), the Ninth Circuit clarified that Rogers remains applicable where a mark is used solely for expressive purposes and not to designate the source of the infringer’s goods. In other words, Rogers is inapplicable only when the alleged infringer uses the mark to identify the source of its own product.

Against that backdrop, the central threshold inquiry in Hara was whether the challenged depiction functioned as a source identifier or merely as part of the show’s expressive content.

The Dispute: Vicky Vox and Q-Force

Drag performer Lance Hara, professionally known as Vicky Vox, sued Netflix over its 2021 animated series Q-Force, a satirical show about underappreciated LGBTQ+ spies. Vox alleged that her likeness was used without authorization in a ten-second West Hollywood bar scene, a teaser trailer, and a promotional image. According to the complaint, a background character with red-orange hair, cat-eye makeup, and a fan “thworp” resembled her.

Vox asserted claims for false endorsement under the Lanham Act, citing inquiries from members of the public about a possible association with the show, as well as state-law right-of-publicity claims. The district court dismissed the federal claims on First Amendment grounds and declined to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over the state claims. Vox appealed.

The Ninth Circuit’s Holding: RogersTest Bars the Claims

The Ninth Circuit concluded that Vox’s claims were governed by the Rogers test. The court reasoned that brief depiction of Vox’s likeness did not identify the source or origin of Q-Force. Instead, it functioned as part of the show’s expressive content. Because the likeness was not used as a trademark or source identifier, Rogers, rather than the likelihood-of-confusion test, applied.

Applying Rogers, the panel held that Vox failed to satisfy either prong. As to artistic relevance, the depiction easily cleared the low “above zero” threshold. The animated drag queen contributed meaningfully to the show’s setting, humor, and cultural commentary, particularly in a scene set in a West Hollywood bar. Turning to the second prong, the court found no overt claims or explicit misrepresentations suggesting that Vox endorsed or was affiliated with the show. Evidence of consumer confusion, including inquiries about a possible connection, was deemed irrelevant because the Rogers analysis focuses on the defendant’s conduct, not the downstream effects of the use.

Because the promotional materials were integral to the expressive work itself, the panel unanimously held that the district court properly dismissed Vox’s Lanham Act claims. 

Guidance for Future Cases

The opinion provides important post-Jack Daniel’s clarification. In the Ninth Circuit, Rogers remains a “shortcut to dismissal” for non–source-identifying uses in expressive works, even when the use suggests affiliation. The central inquiry is whether the mark functions to identify the work’s source or origin. If the mark is instead used solely for other expressive purposes (such as realism, commentary, or satire) Rogers applies, and evidence of consumer confusion or intent will not defeat dismissal absent an explicitly misleading use. This framework draws a clearer line between artistic depiction and commercial branding, offering a more predictable roadmap for evaluating future claims.

Implications for Creative Industries

For creators in animation, film, and television, Hara is a significant development because it confirms that brief, incidental uses of real-life figures for authenticity are generally protected from Lanham Act claims. The decision encourages the use of cultural references to enrich storytelling, provided those references do not function as source identifiers or make explicit claims of endorsement. Satirical and themed works like Q-Force are likely to benefit from this framework, but more prominent or dominant depictions may still warrant closer scrutiny.

At the same time, the ruling does not eliminate all risk. Producers should consult counsel early in development, especially for projects involving celebrities, to assess potential exposure. Overall, the decision reinforces a strong commitment to free expression by favoring protected speech over speculative claims of consumer confusion.
