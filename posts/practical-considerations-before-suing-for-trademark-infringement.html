Practical Considerations Before Suing for Trademark Infringement

Filing a trademark infringement lawsuit can be a powerful way to protect a brand, but it shouldn’t be undertaken without carefully weighing the strategic and practical factors that determine whether litigation is worthwhile.

Alec McNiff · 5 min read

Filing a trademark infringement lawsuit can be a powerful way to protect a brand, but it shouldn’t be undertaken without carefully weighing the strategic and practical factors that determine whether litigation is worthwhile. This article outlines key considerations to review before taking action.

1. Ensure Priority (First to Use)

In the U.S., trademark rights generally go to the party who first uses a mark in commerce. Establishing priority often requires clear documentation of when and how the mark was first used, along with evidence of continuous use. If another party claims concurrent use, the issue may become more complex, particularly in overlapping geographic or product markets. Before filing suit, it’s essential to assess whether you have a strong claim as the first user of the mark. Ensuring priority is critical, because failing to establish it could expose you to a counterclaim for infringement, effectively acknowledging the similarity of the marks.

2. Strength of the Mark

The strength of a mark plays a major role in any infringement analysis. Unsurprisingly, strong marks are easier to enforce and more likely to result in favorable outcomes. Prospective plaintiffs should consider the following factors when evaluating the strength of a mark:

  • Registration: Federal registration provides presumptive nationwide rights and constructive notice to others. Unregistered marks rely on common law rights, which are geographically limited.
  • Incontestability: Marks that have achieved incontestable status enjoy protection against certain challenges, such as descriptiveness claims.
  • Distinctiveness: Arbitrary and fanciful marks are strongest. Suggestive marks are moderately strong. Descriptive marks must have acquired secondary meaning to qualify for protection.
  • Fame and Consumer Recognition: Well-known or famous marks may receive broader protection, including against dilution or similar uses even in unrelated markets.

A weak or descriptive mark may be enforceable, but litigation is riskier and often more expensive relative to likely remedies.

3. Gaps in Scope of Protection

Even strong marks have limits. Before suing, consider whether there are gaps in protection that could affect your case. These gaps might include:

  • Geographic Scope: For unregistered marks, rights may be limited to areas where the mark is actively used.
  • Continuity of Use: Inconsistent or abandoned use can undermine a claim.
  • Industry or Product-Specific Limitations: Rights typically extend only to the goods or services for which the mark is used.

Understanding these limitations helps set realistic expectations for what a lawsuit can achieve.

4. Good Faith vs. Bad Faith Intent

The alleged infringer’s intent can influence remedies and damages, not to mention the public’s perception of the dispute. Courts may consider whether the infringer knowingly copied your mark or acted in bad faith. Although unintentional use does not necessarily defeat an infringement claim, clear evidence of copying or deliberate confusion can strengthen your position, both in court and in the court of public opinion.

5. Potential Defenses

Even if your mark is strong and priority is clear, defendants may raise defenses that could limit or defeat your claim. Assessing these in advance helps you determine the strength of your case and prepare evidence accordingly:

  • Fair Use: Descriptive fair use occurs when the alleged infringer uses the mark in good faith to describe their own goods or services rather than to indicate source. For example, using the word “apple” to describe the fruit rather than the tech company. Nominative fair use allows limited use of a mark to refer to the mark owner’s goods or services, often for comparative advertising or commentary. Both forms can be valid defenses if the use is reasonable and not misleading.
  • Abandonment: As mentioned above, a mark that has not been used consistently or has been deliberately abandoned may no longer be enforceable. Evidence of discontinuous use or intentional non-use can be a strong defense.
  • Laches or Acquiescence: Delay can hurt your case. If you knew about the use and waited too long to enforce your rights, the defendant can argue that the delay caused prejudice. Although laches doesn’t always bar injunctive relief, it can limit damages. Relatedly, acquiescence applies when the owner’s conduct led the defendant to reasonably believe the owner consented to the use. Suggesting collaboration, failing to object during negotiations, or giving mixed signals can fuel this defense.
  • Estoppel: If your statements or conduct misled the defendant into believing they could use the mark, you may be estopped from claiming infringement. This can occur through silence in situations where you had a clear duty to speak, or through prior dealings.

6. Other Practical Considerations

Litigation is costly and time-consuming. Before committing to a lawsuit, it’s worth taking a hard look at whether the juice is worth the squeeze. In many cases, the litigation costs can exceed the likely recovery, especially if damages are uncertain or the infringing use is already tapering off.

Potential plaintiffs should also consider the likelihood of obtaining injunctive relief. Courts are far more willing to issue injunctions when the infringement is clear, ongoing, and likely to mislead consumers. But the expected value of litigation may drop sharply if the case is borderline or the alleged infringer has already backed down.

Finally, one must consider the potential impact on your brand and public reputation. Litigation can sometimes create a Barbra Streisand effect, causing more harm and attention than the underlying infringement ever did. Potential plaintiffs must think about whether a public fight aligns with broader business goals and how it might affect customer relationships, industry partners, or media perception.

In short, not every dispute needs to be litigated. Demand letters, structured negotiations, or mediation often resolve conflicts at a fraction of the cost. Exploring these options early can preserve resources and avoid the unpredictability of the courtroom.

Conclusion

Trademark infringement lawsuits can be powerful tools, but success depends on more than proving infringement. Assessing priority, mark strength, scope of protection, intent, potential defenses, and practical implications will help you decide whether litigation is the right strategy. Thoughtful preparation not only increases your chances of a favorable outcome but can also save time, money, and reputational risk.
