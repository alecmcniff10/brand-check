Rule 40 of the Olympic Charter: An Exception to Ordinary Trademark Law

Rule 40 of the Olympic Charter establishes a distinct regulatory framework governing advertising and promotional activity involving Olympic participants.

Alec McNiff · 5 min read

Rule 40 of the Olympic Charter establishes a distinct regulatory framework governing advertising and promotional activity involving Olympic participants. It restricts the commercial use of athletes’ names, images, likenesses, and sporting performances during the defined “Games period,” which begins shortly before the Opening Ceremony and ends shortly after the Closing Ceremony.

The rule is designed to preserve the exclusivity purchased by official Olympic sponsors and to protect the revenue structure that finances the Olympic Movement. In doing so, it blends trademark principles, contractual commitments imposed on participants, and host-country legal protections. However, it diverges from traditional Lanham Act analysis and conventional trademark doctrine in important ways.

Scope and Operation of Rule 40

Under Rule 40, competitors, team officials, and other team personnel participating in the Olympic Games may permit the use of their person, name, picture, or sports performances for advertising purposes only in accordance with principles established by the International Olympic Committee (IOC) Executive Board. These principles, which have been refined and reissued for successive Games, impose the following core restrictions during the Games period:

  1. Non-sponsor entities may not use Olympic intellectual property, including the Olympic rings, emblems, or protected terminology;
  2. Advertising and promotional activities may not create a direct or indirect association with the Olympic Games;
  3. Campaigns featuring participants must be “generic,” avoiding any reference to Olympic participation or performance; and
  4. In most jurisdictions, campaigns must have launched before the Games period and may not be materially intensified during that time.

Statutory Foundation: Enhanced Rights Recognized by the Supreme Court

The legal foundation for Rule 40’s expansive protections lies in the Amateur Sports Act of 1978, now known as the Ted Stevens Olympic and Amateur Sports Act (the “Act”), as interpreted by the Supreme Court in San Francisco Arts & Athletics, Inc. v. United States Olympic Committee, 483 U.S. 522 (1987). In that case, the Court upheld Congress’s grant of exclusive rights in the word “Olympic” and related symbols to the U.S. Olympic Committee.

The Court confirmed that these rights extend beyond traditional trademark protection. Unlike a standard Lanham Act claim, enforcement under the Act does not require proof of likelihood of confusion. Instead, the Court recognized that Congress granted a broader form of statutory exclusivity that allows the Olympic Committee to prohibit advertising that creates an unauthorized association with the Olympic Games, even if consumers are not confused about source or sponsorship.

For example, a company may lawfully state that it supports a particular athlete. But if its advertising links that support to the timing or location of the Games, such as by referencing “this summer in Paris” or similar cues, it will generally violate Rule 40. The violation stems not from confusion, but from the creation of an impermissible associative link.

The Court went further, however, holding that an unauthorized user of marks protected under the Act may not invoke the ordinary statutory defenses typically available in trademark litigation. Olympic-related marks therefore occupy a uniquely protected position in U.S. advertising law, reflecting Congress’s deliberate choice to safeguard the commercial value of the Games through heightened exclusivity.

Enforcement Mechanisms Under Rule 40

Rule 40 is enforced through a combination of contractual control, institutional oversight, and statutory backing. Unlike typical trademark disputes, enforcement does not depend primarily on private litigation between competitors. Instead, compliance is embedded in the Olympic regulatory structure itself.

First, athletes are contractually bound to comply with Rule 40 as a condition of eligibility. Entry forms, athlete agreements, and National Olympic Committee rules incorporate the Charter by reference. Violations may result in disciplinary measures, including withdrawal of accreditation, disqualification, or other sanctions imposed by the relevant National Olympic Committee (NOC) or the IOC.

Second, the NOCs actively monitor advertising and social media during the Games period. Campaigns that risk creating unauthorized association are often addressed through cease-and-desist communications or demands for modification. In multi-jurisdictional campaigns, coordination may occur between the NOC and the IOC to ensure consistent enforcement.

Third, Rule 40 operates alongside statutory protections in host countries and domestic legislation, such as the Act. Where necessary, these statutes may provide a basis for injunctive relief or other judicial remedies.

Taken together, these mechanisms create a layered enforcement model which includes contractual discipline for participants, regulatory oversight during the Games period, and statutory remedies when formal legal action is required.

Implications for Legal Practice

Counsel advising Olympic athletes, their representatives, non-sponsor brands, and advertising agencies must approach Rule 40 differently from routine trademark clearance. The mere absence of Olympic rings or the word “Olympic” in creative materials does not end the analysis. Instead, counsel must evaluate whether the timing, messaging, or overall context of a campaign could create an impermissible association with the Games during the protected period.

Endorsement and sponsorship agreements involving Olympic participants should therefore expressly address:

  • Clear delineation of the applicable Games period;
  • Restrictions on messaging and required pre-approval processes;
  • Social-media controls, including posting guidelines and monitoring obligations;
  • Notification and pre-clearance procedures with the relevant National Olympic Committee and the IOC (where multi-territory campaigns are involved); and
  • Representations, warranties, and indemnity provisions concerning Rule 40 compliance.

Absent these safeguards, parties risk more than a standard trademark dispute. Potential consequences include athlete discipline, contractual exposure, and injunctive relief.

Conclusion

Rule 40 reflects a deliberate departure from traditional trademark doctrine in service of a distinct commercial objective. By prohibiting unauthorized association rather than requiring proof of confusion, and by combining statutory rights and contractual enforcement, the Olympic Movement has established a distinct exclusivity framework to protect the revenue model that funds the Games.

For practitioners in trademark, advertising, and sports law, this framework underscores that the legal environment governing global sporting events may not map neatly onto traditional intellectual property principles. Effective counseling requires attention not only to trademark doctrine, but also to the specific governance and enforcement structure that shapes how these rights function in practice.
