Sampling, twice removed: the chain-of-title problem nobody is solving.

When a sync license clears the master but not the underlying interpolation, the bill comes due in arbitration.

Alec R. Whitfield · 11 min read

Every music lawyer's least favorite phone call begins with the same six words: "the spot is in market and—." Whatever follows is bad news about a license that should have been cleared three weeks ago.

The structural problem is that modern hit songs increasingly contain layered samples and interpolations of older works. Clearing the recorded master is straightforward. Clearing the underlying composition — and any composition it samples — is not.

The two-tier license, and why it fails

A sync license is conventionally drafted as two parallel agreements: one with the master owner, one with the publisher. The publisher agreement assumes the publisher controls one hundred percent of the composition. When the composition is itself an interpolation, that assumption is wrong.

The publisher signing the sync may control only the new layer. The old layer is somebody else's problem — and now yours.

What the warranty and indemnity clauses actually do

Most sync agreements contain a warranty that the licensor controls all rights necessary to grant the license, and an indemnity backing it up. In practice, both are expensive paper. Indemnity disputes go to arbitration; arbitration takes eighteen months; the spot is on air the entire time.

A drafting fix

Add a chain-of-title rider. It should require the licensor to deliver, within five business days of execution, a chain-of-title certificate identifying every underlying work, every controlled-composition share, and every consent already obtained. Make delivery a condition precedent to the licensee's payment of the advance.

A chain-of-title rider, conditioned on the advance, is the cheapest insurance in entertainment law.

It will not solve every chain-of-title problem. It will solve the ones that begin with a phone call you don't want to receive.
