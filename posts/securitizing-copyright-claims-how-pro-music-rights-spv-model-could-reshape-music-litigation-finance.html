Securitizing Copyright Claims: How Pro Music Rights' SPV Model Could Reshape Music Litigation Finance

Pro Music Rights' attempt to bundle copyright infringement claims and licensing agreements into tradeable securities through a SPV raises questions about standing, assignability, and what investors would actually be buying.

Alec McNiff · 9 min read

Music Licensing, Inc. (OTC: SONG), the parent company of Pro Music Rights, announced on May 11 that it plans to form a Special Purpose Vehicle to bundle its copyright infringement claims and licensing agreements into tradeable asset-backed securities. If the structure works as described, it would be an unusual application of securitization mechanics to copyright infringement claims, an asset class that capital markets have not traditionally touched.

The announcement is heavy on capital markets jargon, but the underlying structure is not complicated: Pro Music Rights claims it holds a large portfolio of licensing agreements and copyright infringement claims that it cannot efficiently convert to cash through traditional collection and litigation. Its proposed solution is to transfer those assets to a Luxembourg-based SPV, which would then issue structured notes and asset-backed securities to investors under SEC Rule 144A (for qualified institutional buyers in the U.S.) and Regulation S (for non-U.S. investors), with listings anticipated on the Luxembourg Stock Exchange and possibly the Vienna Stock Exchange.

What is securitization, and why does it matter?

Securitization is a process by which illiquid assets, such as loans, receivables, or contractual payment streams, are pooled together and transferred to a separate legal entity (the SPV), which then issues securities backed by the cash flows from those assets. The most familiar example is the mortgage-backed security: a bank originates thousands of home loans, transfers them to an SPV, and the SPV issues bonds to investors who receive payments as homeowners make their monthly mortgage payments.

The logic of applying this structure to music copyright claims is that copyright infringement under 17 U.S.C. § 504 carries a statutory damages framework (between $750 and $30,000 per work infringed, and up to $150,000 per work for willful infringement), which gives each claim a theoretically quantifiable range of potential recovery. Licensing agreements, meanwhile, generate contractual payment obligations that look more like traditional receivables. The SPV model promises to convert both into upfront capital by letting investors buy exposure to the expected cash flows.

The developing litigation finance landscape

The idea of financing IP litigation through outside capital is not new. Burford Capital, the largest publicly traded litigation funder, has been investing in patent and IP claims for over a decade. According to a 2024 Government Accountability Office report, most large technology companies reported that more than half of all patent infringement lawsuits filed against them had confirmed or suspected third-party funding, and the industry has grown significantly since gaining a foothold in the U.S. around 2010. The model works because litigation is expensive, outcomes are binary, and many rights holders with strong claims cannot afford to pursue them.

In the music industry, this funding gap is especially acute for independent publishers and smaller rights organizations that lack the legal budgets to pursue sustained enforcement campaigns.

What Pro Music Rights is proposing goes a step beyond traditional litigation finance. Rather than a funder investing in a single case or portfolio of cases in exchange for a share of the recovery, the SPV model would package the claims themselves into financial instruments that trade on secondary markets. That structural gap between traditional litigation funding and securitized claim pools brings its own set of legal complications.

Key legal questions

Securities regulation and disclosure. If the SPV issues notes or bonds backed by contingent litigation claims, those instruments will almost certainly be treated as securities under U.S. law, regardless of whether they are offered under a registration exemption like Rule 144A. The investment contract test under SEC v. W.J. Howey Co., 328 U.S. 293 (1946) looks at whether there is an investment of money in a common enterprise with an expectation of profits derived from the efforts of others, and an SPV that pools litigation claims and pays returns based on enforcement outcomes checks every element. Investors in asset-backed securities typically receive detailed offering memoranda with information about the underlying asset pool, including default rates, recovery assumptions, and concentration risk. The challenge with litigation-backed instruments is that the underlying "assets" are not loans with historical repayment data. They are legal claims with uncertain outcomes. How does an investor model the expected cash flow of a copyright infringement claim that has not yet been filed, let alone adjudicated? The disclosure obligations under Regulation AB or comparable frameworks would require granular information about the claims, and the lack of actuarial data for music copyright litigation makes that a difficult exercise.

Standing, assignability, and the PRO question. Under 17 U.S.C. § 501(b), only the "legal or beneficial owner of an exclusive right under a copyright" has standing to sue for infringement. Federal courts have uniformly held, under the current Copyright Act, that the bare assignment of an accrued infringement claim does not confer standing on the assignee. The Ninth Circuit reached this conclusion in Silvers v. Sony Pictures Entertainment, Inc., 402 F.3d 881 (9th Cir. 2005) (en banc), the Second Circuit followed in John Wiley & Sons, Inc. v. DRK Photo, 882 F.3d 394 (2d Cir. 2018), and the Eleventh Circuit recently reaffirmed this position in Great Bowery Inc. v. Consequence Sound LLC (2026). An older Fifth Circuit decision, Prather v. Neva Paperbacks, Inc., 410 F.2d 698 (5th Cir. 1969), took a more permissive view, but that case was decided under the 1909 Copyright Act and has not been followed under the current statutory framework. The upshot is that an SPV cannot simply acquire bare infringement claims and then sue on them. Standing requires an ownership interest in the copyright itself.

As a PRO, Pro Music Rights does not own the copyrights in the works it represents. The songwriters, composers, and publishers retain ownership; Pro Music Rights collects performance royalties and pursues licensing on their behalf. PROs like ASCAP and BMI operate the same way, and they enforce performance rights against unlicensed venues routinely. In practice, this enforcement works because infringers rarely challenge PRO standing, the underlying infringement is typically clear-cut, and cases almost always settle. But the precise legal basis for PRO standing under § 501(b) is less settled than the enforcement track record might suggest. As one analysis in the University of Chicago Law Review noted, PROs frequently bring infringement actions to protect their members' copyrights, but whether they have standing to do so "is actually an unresolved problem, as courts have never discussed applying associational standing in these situations." In a typical venue enforcement case, this ambiguity does not matter much. In a securitization context, where investors need to model the legal enforceability of the underlying assets, it matters considerably more.

This standing landscape shapes what the SPV can realistically do. The most viable structure would be a servicing model: Pro Music Rights retains its role as the enforcement entity (bringing claims on behalf of or alongside its affiliated rights holders), while the SPV provides capital and receives economic proceeds from recoveries, similar to how mortgage originators continue to service loans after selling them into a securitization. The SPV never needs standing because it never sues. But this structure depends entirely on the continuing capacity and willingness of the originating entity to litigate, which introduces counterparty risk that investors in traditional asset-backed securities do not typically bear. If the servicer becomes insolvent or loses the cooperation of its affiliates, the claims in the pool may become unenforceable regardless of their underlying merit.

The press release also describes securitizing "executed and active licensee agreements generating recurring, contractually obligated royalty and license fee cash flows." Unlike infringement claims, licensing receivables are contractual payment obligations that can be transferred to an SPV without raising standing issues, because enforcement is a matter of contract law rather than copyright litigation. To the extent the SPV functions primarily as a vehicle for securitizing these receivables, the structure rests on firmer legal ground. But the press release does not separate the two asset classes or acknowledge the different legal frameworks that govern them, which makes it difficult for investors to evaluate what they are actually buying.

Who benefits and what to watch

For independent rights holders with large catalogs and limited enforcement budgets, a well-structured SPV model could provide a genuine alternative to the status quo, where claims go unenforced because the cost of litigation exceeds the expected recovery on any individual case. A major publisher or collective rights organization that pooled licensing receivables into an SPV could offer investors something closer to the predictable cash flows they expect from traditional securitizations, because licensing revenue streams are contractual rather than contingent. The infringement side of the equation is harder, but a publisher that actually owns its copyrights (unlike a PRO, which represents them) would be better positioned to transfer the underlying rights necessary to give an SPV enforcement standing.

For attorneys advising clients who are approached to participate in these structures, whether as rights contributors or investors, the due diligence checklist should include: whether the originating entity actually owns the copyrights or merely represents the owners; the standing and assignability framework in the relevant jurisdiction; the quality of the underlying copyright registrations (since timely registration under 17 U.S.C. § 412 is a prerequisite for statutory damages and attorney's fees); and the disclosure and regulatory framework governing the securities being offered.

The litigation finance industry is growing, the music industry's enforcement challenges are well-documented, and capital markets have shown an appetite for increasingly exotic asset classes. Whether or not this particular structure moves forward, the underlying idea of securitizing music licensing receivables is likely to attract further interest. Applying the same mechanics to copyright infringement claims raises harder questions that will need to be addressed.
