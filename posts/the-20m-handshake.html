The $20M handshake: when a college quarterback's likeness outgrows the school that recruited him.

A Big Ten star's jump from a collective deal into a national footwear contract has triggered the messiest right-of-publicity fight since O'Bannon. Inside the contracts, the carve-outs, and the clause every athlete now wishes they had read twice.

Alec R. Whitfield · 14 min read

There is a clause buried halfway through most NIL collective agreements that, until recently, no one read with much care. It assigns to the collective a non-exclusive, royalty-free license to use the athlete's name, image, and likeness "for the purpose of furthering the mission of the collective" — a phrase doing extraordinary work for so few words.

This spring, that clause stopped being theoretical. When a Big Ten quarterback signed a national footwear deal worth a reported $20 million over four years, the collective that had been paying him through his sophomore season filed for declaratory relief in federal district court. They want the contract read to give them a perpetual, non-revocable, transferable license. He wants it read to mean what a reasonable 19-year-old would have thought it meant.

The contract, in three uncomfortable pieces

The agreement at the center of the fight has the shape of a standard collective deal but the content of something much stranger. There are three terms worth pulling out:

First, the grant. It is non-exclusive on its face, but the definition of "use" runs for nearly a page and includes "all media now known or later devised, in all territories, in perpetuity." Whatever the parties intended, the grant looks transferable, and that is the seed of the dispute.

"In all media now known or later devised, in all territories, in perpetuity."

Second, the morality clause — drafted, evidently, by someone who has never met an actual 20-year-old. It permits termination for "any conduct that, in the sole judgment of the collective, brings disrepute upon the program." The collective has not invoked it here, but its presence reframes the entire deal as something other than the equal-arms transaction the athlete's agent has been describing.

Third, and most consequentially, the carve-out. There isn't one. Most professional endorsement contracts carefully reserve the athlete's right to do school-related work, charitable appearances, family use, and so on. This contract does not. The collective's lawyers will argue it didn't have to.

What O'Bannon did and didn't decide

Anyone watching this case is reaching for O'Bannon v. NCAA, and they shouldn't reach too far. O'Bannon was about whether the NCAA could prevent compensation in the first place. It was not about how to allocate rights between athletes and collectives once compensation became permissible.

The NIL era's contract problems are not O'Bannon problems. They are problems we should have seen coming the day after O'Bannon.

The closer analogy is the early years of the music-publishing industry, when artists signed contracts whose ramifications they discovered only when their songs became valuable. The shape of the case the quarterback's lawyers are quietly building looks much more like a Brian Wilson royalty fight than a sports-antitrust appeal.

The clause every athlete now wishes they had read twice

If there is a single sentence in the contract that will decide the case, it is the assignment clause — and specifically a sub-clause permitting "transfer to any successor entity in the event of merger, acquisition, or restructuring." The collective has, in the last twelve months, restructured itself twice. Whether either restructuring was a "merger" within the meaning of the contract is now a question of corporate-law construction the parties did not contemplate at signing.

The lesson for athletes' counsel is uncomfortable but simple: the next generation of NIL contracts needs to be drafted with the assumption that the entity sitting across the table will not be the same entity in three years. That changes how you draft assignment, how you draft termination, and how you draft the survival clause.

The case is set for oral argument on a motion to dismiss next month. We'll be back with what the court actually does with the assignment clause, and what every collective in the country is going to have to redraft in response.
