The Cost of Ignoring Music Licenses: Behr's Case

In November 2025, ABKCO Music & Records, Inc. (owner of the Rolling Stones’ pre-1971 catalog) filed a federal copyright infringement lawsuit against Behr Paint in the Central District of California.

Alec McNiff · 3 min read

In November 2025, ABKCO Music & Records, Inc. (owner of the Rolling Stones’ pre-1971 catalog) filed a federal copyright infringement lawsuit against Behr Paint in the Central District of California. The claim stems from a 15-second Instagram Reel posted by Behr that used the original 1966 Rolling Stones recording of “Paint It, Black” to promote a new line of black paint. No synchronization license and no master-use license were obtained.

The Lawsuit

In late October 2025, Behr posted an Instagram Reel promoting their new shades of black paint. To match the edgy vibe, Behr played the full original 1966 recording of “Paint It, Black,” while showing paint slathered on walls in dramatic black. According to the complaint, Behr never requested or obtained a synchronization license for the underlying musical composition or a master-use license for the sound recording itself. The Reel garnered thousands of views before Behr took it down, just hours after receiving a cease-and-desist letter from ABKCO.

ABKCO alleges direct copyright infringement and characterizes the violation as willful, pointing out that Behr is a multi-billion-dollar company (owned by Masco and closely tied to Home Depot) that routinely licenses well-known music for its advertising campaigns and therefore cannot credibly claim ignorance of the licensing process. In the alternative, the complaint asserts vicarious and contributory infringement against Behr and unnamed Doe defendants (likely the agency or production partners).

Although Behr removed the Reel within hours of receiving ABKCO’s cease-and-desist letter, ABKCO is pressing forward with the case, arguing that the unauthorized commercial use created an unwanted brand association and deprived the rights holders of licensing revenue they otherwise would have earned. ABKCO is seeking the full range of remedies available under the Copyright Act: actual damages and disgorgement of Behr’s profits attributable to the infringement (17 U.S.C. § 504(b)), or alternatively the maximum statutory damages for willful infringement (up to $150,000 per work under § 504(c)), plus attorneys’ fees, costs, and prejudgment interest.

Takeaways for Brand and Marketing Teams

  1. Two separate licenses are always required to use a famous original recording: Music copyright is divided into two distinct rights: the musical composition (lyrics + melody) and the sound recording (the specific studio master). Using the actual Rolling Stones recording of “Paint It, Black” legally requires (a) a synchronization (“sync”) license from the music publisher and (b) a master-use license from the owner of the recording (ABKCO in this case). Securing only one of these licenses (or none at all) is still infringement.
  1. Stock-music and subscription libraries do not include major-label masters: Platforms such as Epidemic Sound, Artlist, AudioJungle, MusicBed, APM, Killer Tracks, and similar services are excellent for royalty-free or pre-cleared production music, but they never contain the original studio recordings of superstar artists released by Universal, Sony, Warner, or legacy catalogs like ABKCO. You will find only newly created tracks or cover versions in those libraries. There is no legal mechanism for those services to offer the real 1966 Rolling Stones master (or virtually any classic-rock radio staple) at scale. Assuming otherwise is a common and expensive mistake.
  1. “We took it down right away” is not a defense: Duration and speed of removal can sometimes reduce damages and demonstrate good faith, but they do not eliminate liability. Federal courts regularly award significant statutory damages for brief, commercial uses of even a few seconds of a famous recording when the infringement is deemed willful.
  1. Always get written confirmation of exactly which rights were cleared: The single most common cause of an infringement lawsuit like this one is a breakdown in the chain of responsibility: the agency clears the composition but assumes the brand will handle the master, the social team uses the original Spotify rip because “it sounded better,” or a freelancer says “the music is licensed” without specifying which rights. Companies can eliminate the ambiguity by requiring every vendor, agency, or contractor to confirm in writing (before the content is published) that both the sync license and the master-use license have been obtained for the exact recording used, and to attach copies of both approvals. If they cannot provide both documents, do not post the work.

Clearing music properly at the outset almost always costs a fraction of what a federal copyright lawsuit will cost to litigate or settle. The Behr case is a reminder that even large, sophisticated companies can make costly mistakes when they skip the music-licensing step.
