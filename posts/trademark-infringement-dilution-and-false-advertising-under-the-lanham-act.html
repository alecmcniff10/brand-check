Trademark Infringement, Dilution, and False Advertising Under the Lanham Act

The Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. § 1051 et seq .) is the primary federal statute governing trademarks and unfair competition. It provides protections against three distinct but related harms: trademark infringement, trademark dilution, and false advertising.

Alec McNiff · 6 min read

The Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. § 1051 et seq.) is the primary federal statute governing trademarks and unfair competition. It provides protections against three distinct but related harms: trademark infringement, trademark dilution, and false advertising. All three aim to promote fair competition and protect both consumers and brand owners, but they address different types of misconduct. Trademark infringement focuses on consumer confusion, dilution protects the uniqueness of famous marks, and false advertising targets deceptive commercial statements. Understanding their elements, differences, and appropriate applications is crucial for businesses navigating brand protection.

Trademark Infringement

Trademark infringement occurs when an unauthorized party uses a mark (or a confusingly similar one) in a way that is likely to cause consumer confusion about the source, sponsorship, or affiliation of goods or services. This claim protects any valid trademark (registered or unregistered) from uses that mislead the public.

The core elements of a federal trademark infringement claim under Sections 32 (for registered marks) or 43(a) (for unregistered marks) of the Lanham Act are:

  1. The plaintiff owns a valid, protectable trademark;
  2. The plaintiff has priority (i.e., used the mark first); and
  3. The defendant’s use of a similar mark in connection with the sale of goods or services is likely to cause confusion among consumers.

See 15 U.S.C. § 1114(1)(a); 15 U.S.C. § 1125(a)(1)(A). For infringement claims, likelihood of confusion is key. This is assessed through multi-factor analyses such as the Polaroid factors (in the Second circuit) or the Sleekcraft factors (in the Ninth Circuit). Common factors, which courts weigh holistically, include strength of the plaintiff’s mark, degree of similarity between the marks, similarity of the goods or services, evidence of actual confusion, marketing channels, defendant’s intent, and buyer sophistication.

Remedies for infringement include injunctions, damages (actual or profits), attorney’s fees in exceptional cases, and destruction of infringing goods.

Trademark Dilution

Dilution protects famous trademarks from uses that weaken their distinctiveness or harm their reputation, even absent confusion or competition. Enacted by the Federal Trademark Dilution Act (1995) and revised by the Trademark Dilution Revision Act (2006) under Section 43(c) of the Lanham Act, dilution targets the whittling away of a mark’s selling power. 

Elements for a federal dilution claim include:

  1. The plaintiff’s mark is famous (i.e., is widely recognized by the general public, determined by factors like duration/extent of use, advertising, sales volume, and actual recognition);
  2. The defendant uses a similar mark in commerce;
  3. The defendant’s use began after the plaintiff’s mark became famous; and
  4. The use is likely to cause dilution.

Jada Toys, Inc. v. Mattel, Inc., 518 F.3d 628, 634 (9th Cir. 2008). Dilution comes in two forms: dilution by blurring, and dilution by tarnishment. 15 U.S.C. § 1125(c)(1). Blurring has to do with an association that weakens the mark’s distinctiveness. This occurs when the similar mark reduces the public’s immediate connection between the famous mark and its unique source. In analyzing claims for blurring, courts consider factors like similarity, distinctiveness of the famous mark, exclusivity of use, degree of recognition, intent to associate, and actual association. Classic examples include “Buick aspirin” or “Kodak pianos.” See Mead Data Cent., Inc. v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc., 875 F.2d 1026, 1031 (2d Cir. 1989). Although these are unlikely to confuse consumers about the source, the famous mark’s distinctiveness is diminished.

Dilution by tarnishment, on the other hand, is about an association that harms the famous mark’s reputation. This may occur by linking the famous mark to inferior or unsavory products. For example, the Second Circuit held that a defendant’s poster reading “Enjoy Cocaine,” styled in lettering and colors similar to Coca-Cola’s iconic “Enjoy Coca-Cola” mark, could tarnish Coca-Cola and harm its business reputation. Coca-Cola Co. v. Gemini Rising, Inc., 346 F. Supp. 1183, 1191-92 (E.D.N.Y. 1972).

Unlike infringement, proof of confusion, competition, or actual economic injury is unnecessary for dilution claims. The likelihood of dilution alone is sufficient to obtain remedies, which primarily include injunctions. Monetary damages can also be awarded if the defendant willfully intended to trade on the owner’s reputation or to cause dilution. 15 U.S.C. § 1125(c)(5).

False Advertising

False advertising, under Section 43(a)(1)(B) of the Lanham Act, prohibits misleading statements in commercial advertising or promotion. It allows competitors (or others with standing) to challenge deceptive claims that harm their interests.

Key elements of false advertising include:

  1. The defendant made a false or misleading statement of fact in commercial advertising or promotion;
  2. The statement actually deceives or has a tendency to deceive a substantial portion of the audience (literal falsity needs no extrinsic evidence; implied falsity often requires consumer surveys);
  3. The deception is material (i.e., likely to influence purchasing decisions);
  4. The advertised goods or services traveled in interstate commerce; and
  5. The plaintiff has been or is likely to be injured (e.g., lost sales or reputational harm).

Southland Sod Farms v. Stover Seed Co., 108 F.3d 1134, 1139 (9th Cir. 1997). Standing requires the plaintiff to be within the “zone of interest” by establishing commercial injury such as lost sales or damage to business reputation. Lexmark Intern., Inc. v. Static Control Components, Inc., 572 U.S. 118 (2014). Remedies are similar to those for infringement, including injunctions, damages, and potentially the cost of corrective advertising. See, e.g., Larry Pitt & Assoc. v. Lundy Law LLP, 294 F.Supp.3d 329 (2018).

Choosing the Right Claims

Trademark infringement, dilution, and false advertising differ significantly in scope and requirements. Understanding those differences helps clarify when each claim is most appropriate.

Infringement protects any valid trademark, famous or not, and focuses on the likelihood of consumer confusion as the primary harm. It typically arises in competitive or related markets where similar marks on similar goods or services could mislead buyers about origin or affiliation. In practice, infringement is the go-to for most trademark disputes because it directly addresses market diversion and allows successful plaintiffs to recover damages or lost profits.

Dilution, by contrast, applies only to famous marks and protects against harm to a mark’s distinctiveness or reputation, regardless of consumer confusion or direct competition. Unlike infringement or false advertising, where competition is often relevant, dilution can occur even between entirely unrelated products, because the focus is on either blurring the mark’s uniqueness or tarnishing its image. Dilution claims are particularly suitable when the mark’s prestige or uniqueness is threatened without any likelihood of confusion. For example, in Nike, Incorporated v. Nikepal International, Incorporated, Nike successfully proved dilution by blurring based on Nikepal’s use of “NIKEPAL” for laboratory products and services completely unrelated to apparel. 2007 WL 2782030 (E.D. Cal. Sept. 18, 2007). The court emphasized the similar identity of the marks, the extraordinary fame and distinctiveness of NIKE, and survey evidence showing an 87% association rate among relevant consumers, and entered a permanent injunction barring use of the NIKEPAL mark. Id. at *8. 

False advertising stands apart because it does not require any trademark involvement. It targets deceptive statements in promotion, where the primary harm is consumer deception that influences purchasing decisions rather than confusion about source. While infringement and dilution both involve mark usage, false advertising can involve purely factual misrepresentations (literal or implied) completely unrelated to branding. Thus, false advertising is used for deceptive claims without mark misuse (e.g., a competitor falsely claiming “clinically proven” superiority). 

The distinctions among infringement, dilution, and false advertising shape both litigation strategy and risk assessment. Each claim targets a different type of competitive harm, and misalignment between theory and facts can weaken even strong brands’ positions. Evaluating these claims early, with careful attention to evidence and jurisdiction, helps brand owners safeguard their marks and make informed strategic choices.
