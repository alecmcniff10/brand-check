Trademarking the Athlete: How NIL Has Transformed the USPTO Into a Sports Law Battlefield

The USPTO has been on a quiet campaign to reach athletes. Here is what practitioners need to know about the filings that have followed.

Alec McNiff · 4 min read

The USPTO has been on a quiet campaign to reach athletes. Over the past year, the agency launched a dedicated NIL website, hosted webinars for college athletes on trademark basics, and in April sent a team to the 2026 NFL Draft in Pittsburgh with an education booth and a panel featuring USPTO Director John Squires. The message was straightforward: if you are using your name, image, or likeness commercially, federal trademark registration is a tool you should know about.

The campaign reflects a shift with roots all the way back to 2021, when the NCAA lifted its NIL restrictions following the Supreme Court's unanimous ruling in NCAA v. Alston, 594 U.S. 69 (2021). Once athletes could sign endorsement deals and sell branded merchandise, thousands of them had a reason to think about their names as commercial assets, and trademark filings followed. Some of those filings reflect a clear strategy, covering multiple goods and services. Others are narrower than they probably should be. The USPTO has positioned trademark registration as a practical complement to the patchwork of state-level publicity rights that currently governs NIL protection, and it wants applicants to show up better prepared.

What athletes can (and cannot) trademark

Filing a trademark application does not automatically give an athlete exclusive rights over their own name. To warrant trademark protection, a mark must function as a source identifier, meaning it must indicate to consumers that a particular person is the source of a specific good or service. An athlete's name on a jersey is not the same as an athlete's name on a line of branded merchandise they are selling, and only the latter is likely to support a federal registration.

Examples of categories of marks athletes commonly seek to protect include personal names and nicknames, logos and stylized signatures, silhouettes and other distinctive imagery, and catchphrases associated with an athlete's public persona.

One procedural issue worth flagging is that any trademark application that includes the name, portrait, or signature of a living individual requires written consent under Section 2(c) of the Lanham Act. When an athlete files for their own name, consent is presumed. If a third party attempts to register an athlete's name without consent, Section 2(c) provides a basis to block that registration.

The thin-filing problem

Although filing for some protection is better than none, athletes who file applications should be sure to include all relevant categories. Many athletes' applications cover only a single product category, often athletic apparel. That may reflect what the athlete is currently doing, but trademark rights are tied to the goods and services identified in the application. If a third party starts selling branded products in a category the athlete never claimed, the federal registration may not provide the enforcement tool they expected.

That said, an applicant cannot claim categories speculatively. Under Section 1(b) of the Lanham Act, a trademark application can only cover goods or services the applicant has a bona fide intent to offer. But athletes with realistic commercial plans across multiple categories — including live appearances, digital content, merchandise beyond clothing, endorsement services, and potentially food and beverage products — should be thinking about that coverage from the outset. A single application for "athletic apparel" is a good starting point, but not a substitute for a more comprehensive strategy.

Best practices for practitioners

A few principles are worth emphasizing for attorneys counseling athletes on NIL trademark strategy.

First, file early. The cost of a trademark application is relatively modest, and the benefits of establishing a priority date are significant. Athletes whose profiles are about to rise — whether because of a draft, a transfer, or a breakout season — should have applications on file before the public attention arrives. Waiting until after a name becomes commercially valuable invites third-party filings and complicates enforcement.

Second, think beyond apparel. The goods and services listed in an application define the boundaries of the federal registration. Advise clients on what categories align with realistic commercial plans, not just what they are doing today.

Third, anticipate refusals. Surname refusals, failure-to-function refusals, and likelihood-of-confusion refusals are all common in this space. Applicants with common names face particular challenges, and the strength of the response to an office action often determines whether the application proceeds to registration.

Fourth, do not treat trademark registration as a substitute for NIL protection. Trademark law protects a mark as a source identifier. It does not provide a general right to control all commercial uses of an athlete's name or likeness. State right-of-publicity laws and contractual protections remain critical components of a comprehensive NIL strategy.

The USPTO's growing emphasis on NIL education signals that the agency expects continued growth in athlete trademark filings and wants applicants to be better prepared when they arrive. For practitioners, that creates both an opportunity and a responsibility: to help athletes build durable trademark portfolios rather than one-off filings that leave their most valuable assets exposed.
