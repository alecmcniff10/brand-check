Two Quarterback Trademark Strategies Signal the Future of Athlete Branding

On March 16, Caleb Williams Holding Inc. filed four trademark applications with the USPTO. Eleven days later, Fernando Mendoza filed two of his own.

Alec McNiff · 3 min read

On March 16, Caleb Williams Holding Inc. filed four trademark applications with the USPTO. Eleven days later, Fernando Mendoza filed two of his own. Together, these filings represent a new generation of NFL quarterbacks treating intellectual property as a core part of their business, even in its infancy.

Williams, the Bears' rising third-year quarterback, filed on an intent-to-use basis for two marks tied to the nickname “Iceman,” which he earned during a 2025 season that featured six fourth-quarter comebacks and an electric wild card win over Green Bay. His other two applications protect a silhouette of him mid-throw, based on an improbable fourth-and-eight conversion during that playoff game. His filings cover a wide range of goods and services, including eyewear, sunglasses, downloadable digital trading cards, apparel, athletic sporting goods, footballs, water bottles, mugs, bags, and more. These are not Williams’ first trademark filing, as he already has applications on record for his name, a “CW” logo, a bear claw logo, and additional silhouettes. In total, Williams now has eleven trademark applications either pending or in process at the USPTO.

Mendoza, the reigning Heisman and presumptive No. 1 pick in this month’s draft, filed two applications on March 27 covering his full name and his surname for athletic apparel. Although the filings are narrower in scope than Williams’, the instinct to protect his brand early is the same.

The business case for filing early

The traditional path for professional athletes was to build a career, let the endorsement deals come to you, and maybe think about trademarks later, if at all. Players filing trademarks during their playing careers—let alone before even beginning their professional careers—just wasn’t something that was normal. The brand belonged to the league, the team, or whoever was paying for the endorsement.

That model left value on the table, and athletes exposed. If someone else filed first, the player was forced to either buy back rights or fight an expensive legal battle to reclaim them. 

Williams and Mendoza are taking a more proactive approach by filing before their commercial value fully materializes. This secures a federal priority date, which is the legal equivalent of planting a flag. For Williams, that means staking a claim to “Iceman” while the nickname is still fresh and before third parties try to capitalize on it. For Mendoza, it means protecting his name before he goes from college quarterback to the face of an NFL franchise, a transition that will generate even more commercial interest than he already has.

What this means going forward

Trademark protection is no longer an afterthought reserved for players with existing lucrative shoe deals and production companies. Instead, it’s becoming routine for elite prospects and young professionals alike, and will likely trickle down into the college game, as athletes seek to maximize the value of their brand through NIL deals. Going forward, the athletes who treat their IP the way they treat their training, with intention, professional guidance, and an eye toward long-term value, will be the ones best positioned to control their commercial futures. By contrast, those who wait will end up paying more, fighting harder, and owning less.
