Two stripes, three stripes, and the survey that sank a streetwear giant.

A Ninth Circuit panel tells us — yet again — that consumer confusion is a question of evidence, not vibes.

Alec R. Whitfield · 9 min read

In a Lanham Act case, the survey is the case. Last week's Ninth Circuit affirmance is a useful reminder that this is not a metaphor.

The opinion runs forty-three pages, but the part that matters is on page nineteen, where the panel reviews a Squirt-format consumer-confusion survey and concludes — with what reads like a small amount of judicial relief — that it is methodologically sound.

What the survey actually showed

The survey put 412 respondents in front of two side-by-side product images: the senior user's three-stripe athletic shoe and the junior user's two-stripe streetwear lookalike. After filtering out non-purchasers and non-recognizers, 38% of remaining respondents either named the senior user or said the products came from the same company.

"38% net confusion is not a close case. It is a finding that resolves itself."

The defense's expert tried, gamely, to argue that the survey suffered from order-effect bias and a leading screening question. The district court found her testimony unpersuasive. The Ninth Circuit found no clear error in that finding. End of story.

What the case actually teaches

Three things, in descending order of importance.

First, if you are defending a trade-dress case in 2026 and your client cannot afford a survey, you should be settling the case. The Ninth Circuit's deference to the trial court's factfinding on survey methodology means the survey almost always wins.

Second, the Sleekcraft factors are alive and well, but they are not doing the work people think they are doing. The opinion runs through all eight factors, but the analysis is essentially a wrapper around the survey result. The other factors are scenery.

In 2026, the Sleekcraft factors are scenery. The survey is the play.

Third, and most usefully for in-house counsel: when you are clearing a new mark or a new piece of trade dress, run a small consumer-perception study yourself. The cost has dropped to four figures for a directionally useful read. The cost of finding out you were wrong in litigation has not.
