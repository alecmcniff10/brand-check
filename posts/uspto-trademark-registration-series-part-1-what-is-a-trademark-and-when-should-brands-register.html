USPTO Trademark Registration Series: Part 1 - What Is a Trademark and When Should Brands Register?

Although timelines can vary based on individual circumstances, as of January 2026, the average time from filing to first examining action is about 4.6 months, and from filing to registration (or abandonment) is around 10.5 months. These timelines reflect recent improvements in USPTO efficiency, but factors like application volume and complexity can extend them. Subsequent articles will dive deeper into timelines, but for now, I’ll start at the beginning: understanding trademarks and whether federal registration makes sense for your brand.

What Is a Trademark?

A trademark is any word, phrase, symbol, design, or combination thereof that identifies and distinguishes the source of goods or services from those of others. Common examples include words or names (like “Nike” for athletic apparel or “Google” for search engines), logos (such as the Apple logo for electronics), slogans (like “I’m Lovin’ It”) and even non-traditional elements like sounds (think the MGM lion roar), colors (like Tiffany blue for jewelry boxes), or product shapes (such as the Coca-Cola bottle contour).

Trademarks protect brand identity and help consumers avoid confusion in the marketplace. As discussed in this prior article, they don’t protect ideas, inventions, or artistic works (those fall under patents or copyrights), but rather the commercial branding associated with goods or services.

Federal registration with the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) isn’t required to have trademark rights, but it offers significant advantages. Without registration, you may still have “common law” rights based on actual use in commerce, but these are limited geographically and harder to enforce.

Benefits of Federal USPTO Registration

Registering your trademark with the USPTO provides robust protections beyond state-level registration or common-law rights. Key benefits include:

  • Nationwide protection: Constructive priority across the U.S. from your filing date (Section 7(c), Lanham Act), as long as you use the mark in interstate commerce. (But note: this right yields to senior common law users who established rights in specific geographic areas before your filing date; they retain rights in their territories, while you gain superiority elsewhere and usually block their expansion.
  • Presumptive rights: Registration creates a legal presumption of ownership and exclusive rights, making it easier to prove your case in court.
  • Incontestability after 5 years: Once registered for five continuous years (with proper maintenance filings), your mark becomes “incontestable,” limiting the grounds on which others can challenge it.
  • Enhanced enforcement: You can sue for infringement in federal court, seek treble damages for willful violations, and use the ® symbol to deter copycats.
  • Customs protection: The U.S. Customs and Border Protection can block imports of infringing goods.
  • Basis for international protection: A U.S. registration can serve as a foundation for filings in other countries via treaties like the Madrid Protocol.

Differences Between Common Law Rights, State Registration, and Federal USPTO Registration

Trademark rights in the U.S. arise in layers, and understanding the differences can help brand owners assess the right level of protection.

Common law rights: These arise automatically when you start using a mark in connection with the sale of goods or services. They’re free and immediate but typically limited to the geographic area where you’ve established use. For instance, if you sell “BrewMaster” coffee only in California, your common-law rights might prevent a local imitator, but not one in New York. Enforcing common-law rights can be burdensome because the owner must prove prior use, which typically requires significant evidence and is costly. 

State registration: Many states offer trademark registration for marks used within their borders. State registration is typically cheaper and faster than federal, but protection is state-specific. While it’s useful for local businesses, it doesn’t provide nationwide rights or the same legal presumptions that federal registration offers. 

Federal USPTO registration: This is the strongest option for broader operations. It requires demonstrating use (or intent to use) in interstate commerce and passing USPTO examination. Although more involved, it unlocks the benefits mentioned above. Unlike common law or state options, it involves a public record search and opposition period, reducing the likelihood of future disputes.

In short, common law rights are passive and limited, state registration adds some local credibility, and federal registration is typically the best option for national or international growth.

When to Pursue Federal Registration vs. Alternatives

Federal registration is not always necessary right away. The decision depends on the company’s current stage and long-term goals. Registration usually makes sense when a company is operating or planning to operate in interstate commerce, such as through online sales or franchise expansion, when your mark is a valuable asset that is likely to be copied (such as a distinctive logo for a tech startup), or when the brand is being built with an eye toward licensing or eventual sale. 

By contrast, businesses that remain purely local, such as a neighborhood coffee shop, may reasonably rely on common-law rights or a state registration at the start, particularly if budgets are tight and the risk of conflict is low. Federal filing can also be done on an intent-to-use basis, allowing companies to secure priority before launch.

So, for example, a software company planning a nationwide launch of an app called “FitTrack” would likely benefit from filing early to lock in nationwide rights. By contrast, a family-owned neighborhood bakery with a single local location, no online sales, and no expansion plans may have little reason to register at the outset, or at all. That said, delay does not come without risk. Trademark priority often rewards the first to file or use, and a later conflict with a third-party registrant can force costly infringement claims or rebranding. 

Key Considerations and Legal Aspects

Before proceeding with registration, brand owners should assess several key factors under the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. § 1051 et seq.) to determine whether a mark is eligible for protection. As previously discussed here, the USPTO assesses distinctiveness along a spectrum. On that spectrum, fanciful or arbitrary marks, such as invented terms like “Kodak” or unrelated words like “Apple” for computers, receive the strongest protection. Suggestive marks are registrable but offer less protection than fanciful or arbitrary ones. Descriptive marks require proof of acquired secondary meaning to be protectable. And generic terms are never protectable. Identifying the type of mark is important because it directly affects whether and how easily a brand can register its mark, and how strong the resulting rights will be.

Choosing not to register leaves a brand with fewer enforcement options, weaker priority in disputes, and limited ability to stop nationwide infringers. It also increases the risk of inadvertently infringing another party’s rights, which can lead to cease-and-desist demands or litigation. 

Procedural and cost considerations also matter. Expenses include base filing fees of $350 per class of goods or services (effective January 2025), potential surcharges for deficient applications, attorney fees, clearance search costs, and ongoing maintenance filings. Best practice for brand owners is to conduct thorough clearance searches early, remain alert to absolute bars to registration, such as deceptive or scandalous marks under Section 2(a), and ensure they can substantiate use in interstate commerce. Doing so helps avoid investing significant time and resources into a brand that ultimately cannot be protected, and allows brand owners to address potential obstacles before locking in marketing or expansion plans.

Taken together, federal registration is best viewed as a strategic investment rather than a formality. For growing brands, careful upfront evaluation of whether and when to register can prevent unnecessary expense and enforcement gaps. If the decision is to register, brand owners should next turn to key pre-filing steps, including a comprehensive search and clearance analysis, which will be the focus of the next article in this series.
