USPTO Trademark Registration Series: Part 2 – Trademark Clearance 

This is the second installment in a seven-part series on the USPTO trademark registration process. The first article discussed what trademarks are, the differences between common-law, state, and federal rights, and when federal registration makes strategic sense.

Alec McNiff · 9 min read

This is the second installment in a seven-part series on the USPTO trademark registration process. The first article discussed what trademarks are, the differences between common-law, state, and federal rights, and when federal registration makes strategic sense. This article addresses the next step in the process: trademark clearance. Before investing in a brand mark, the business should determine whether the mark is available for use and registration, and whether using it could expose the business to legal risk. 

Trademark clearance is one of the most important risk-management steps in the trademark process. It helps identify potential conflicts early, ultimately saving time, money, and avoidable headaches. 

Why Pre-Filing Clearance Matters

Conducting a trademark search before filing serves several related purposes. 

First, it helps identify existing marks that could block registration at the USPTO. An examining attorney at the USPTO will refuse registration via an office action if a prior mark creates a “likelihood of confusion” under Section 2(d) of the Lanham Act. Many office actions are avoidable if a proper search is conducted beforehand. Similarly, third parties can oppose an application during publication or later seek cancellation based on prior trademark rights. A thorough search often uncovers those risks in advance, before time and money are spent on a filing that is likely to face opposition.

Second, a thorough search helps assess the risk that another party could claim your use infringes its rights, even if registration is technically possible. Trademark rights in the United States are based primarily on use, not registration. A senior user with common-law rights can bring an infringement claim even if it never registered its mark. Failing to uncover those rights before launch can expose a business to cease-and-desist demands, damages claims, or injunctions forcing an abrupt rebrand.

Third, it allows businesses to make informed decisions early, when course corrections are still relatively inexpensive. Rebranding costs extend well beyond legal fees and often include lost goodwill, redesigned packaging, website and domain changes, customer confusion, and wasted marketing spend. Those costs compound quickly, and early clearance reduces the risk of having to undo those investments after a brand is already established. 

Types of Clearance Searches

Trademark searches are not one-size-fits-all. The appropriate scope depends on the brand’s risk tolerance, budget, and growth plans. In practice, searches fall along a spectrum. 

Searches will typically begin with a preliminary or “knockout” search, which is a high-level check designed to identify obvious conflicts. Knockout searches often involve searching the USPTO database for identical or near-identical marks in the same or closely related goods or services. In addition to being quick and inexpensive, they can eliminate clearly unavailable marks early in the naming process. However, they are not sufficient to accurately assess infringement risk.

Comprehensive searches are broader searches designed to uncover both registrability issues and potential infringement exposure. These typically include federal registrations and applications (via the USPTO), state trademark databases (through individual state databases or aggregated services), common-law sources such as business directories and web use, and domain name and social media platforms (such as Google, GoDaddy, X, Instagram, or TikTok). For businesses with international ambitions, foreign trademark databases may also be relevant, particularly in jurisdictions where use is not required to establish rights.

While preliminary searches answer the question, “is this obviously unavailable,” comprehensive searches address the more difficult question, “how risky is it to use and register this mark?”

Conducting the Search

Applicants can perform basic searches on their own using free tools. But for more thorough clearance, especially with complex marks or where the company anticipates expanding internationally, third-party databases, professional search services, or the assistance of a trademark attorney are often advisable. These resources can better identify and evaluate less obvious risks.

  1. Conducting Searches Using USPTO Tools

The USPTO’s trademark database is the starting point for most clearance searches. As part of a knockout search, users can run simple word mark searches to identify exact matches. While helpful, this approach is incomplete, because trademark examination does not require identical marks or identical goods or services. Similarity in sound, appearance, or meaning can be sufficient to support a refusal.

More effective searching uses structured queries and filters. The USPTO’s TMsearch platform offers drop-down search options that allow users to filter results by criteria such as mark status (live versus dead), mark type (standard character versus design), and goods and services classes. These filters are useful for narrowing large result sets and eliminating clearly irrelevant records, such as abandoned applications. However, drop-down searches primarily refine which records are shown, not how the search is performed. 

Field tag searches go a step further by controlling which parts of each record are searched, making them better suited for assessing substantive clearance risk. Each record contains distinct fields, including the mark itself, the owner name, and the goods and services description. Basic keyword searches scan across multiple fields at once, which can generate irrelevant results or overlook meaningful conflicts. By contrast, field tag searching narrows the inquiry to specific fields, which produces more targeted results and better reflects how examining attorneys assess likelihood of confusion.

For example, when evaluating a proposed mark like “FitTrack,” a user might first run a search limited to the mark field for terms such as “FitTrack,” “Fit Trak,” and “FitTrak” to capture spelling and phonetic variations. The user could then run a separate search limited to goods and services covering fitness software, wearable technology, or health-tracking applications, even if the mark wording is different. Reviewing both sets of results together helps identify marks that are similar in name, marks that operate in the same commercial space, and combinations of the two. This provides a more realistic assessment of potential conflicts than a single broad search.

Although powerful, the USPTO database reflects only federally registered and applied-for marks. It does not capture state registrations or common-law use, and it does not evaluate infringement risk. Because of this it is typically only a starting point, and search results must still be analyzed through a legal lens, as discussed more below.

  1. Checking State Registrations and Common-Law Sources

Because trademark rights in the United States can arise through use alone, clearance should also include searching state and common-law sources. State trademark registrations are typically searchable through each state’s secretary of state website, such as California’s. While state registrations offer narrower protection than federal registrations, they can still indicate senior rights within a particular geographic area.

Common-law searching focuses on whether a mark is already in use, regardless of registration status. This often includes reviewing business name databases (such as Dun & Bradstreet or Yelp), trade directories, online marketplaces (like Amazon, eBay, or Etsy), app stores (Google Play and Apple App Store), news archives, and general internet searches using engines like Google or Bing. These sources are especially important for services, local businesses, and newer brands that may rely entirely on unregistered rights.

  1. Investigating Domain Names and Social Media Use

Domain name and social media availability provide additional context in a clearance analysis. Checking domain registrars such as GoDaddy can reveal whether a corresponding .com or other relevant domain is already in use. Similarly, multi-platform username checkers like BrandSnag or others allow users to check handle availability across major social media platforms (including Instagram, X, Facebook, TikTok, YouTube, and others). An unavailable domain or handle does not automatically establish trademark rights, but it can be a useful indicator of existing use that warrants further investigation.

Taken together, state, common-law, domain, and social media searches help fill gaps left by federal databases. While none of these sources alone determines registrability or infringement risk, they often surface real-world uses that materially affect clearance decisions and help avoid conflicts that would otherwise be missed.

  1. Third-Party Databases and Professional Search Services

While individual searches across federal, state, common-law, and online sources can identify many conflicts, comprehensive clearance often requires third-party search providers that aggregate these data sets into a single report. These services can identify business names, domain registrations, app titles, and marketplace listings that would not appear in USPTO records.

Even professional search reports do not replace legal analysis. They provide raw data, which must be interpreted by evaluating similarity, priority, and the practical likelihood of conflict. That said, comprehensive searches are often highly valuable for brands planning regional, national, or international expansion, or where the cost of rebranding would be high.

Analyzing Search Results for Confusingly Similar Marks

Finding prior marks is only the first step. The critical question is whether any of those marks present a meaningful risk of confusion. The USPTO and courts evaluate the likelihood of confusion using a multi-factor test derived from the DuPont factors, as established in In re E.I. du Pont de Nemours & Co., 476 F.2d 1357 (C.C.P.A. 1973). No single factor is dispositive. Examining attorneys and courts weigh them based on the evidence in each case. The most commonly emphasized factors include:

  • Similarity of the marks: The marks are compared in their entireties for appearance, sound, connotation, and commercial impression. Small differences (e.g., “ByteGuard” vs. “BiteGuard”) may not suffice if the overall impression is similar, particularly in sound or meaning.
  • Relatedness of the goods or services: Goods or services need not be identical or competitive; it is enough if they are related in a way that consumers might reasonably believe they come from the same source (e.g., computer software and related hardware, or auto parts distributorship services and service station oil change and lubrication services) See, e.g., In re Shell Oil Co., 992 F.2d 1204, 1207 (Fed. Cir. 1993). Complementary products or those sold through similar channels heighten the risk.
  • Strength of the prior mark: Stronger, more distinctive marks (fanciful or arbitrary, like “Kodak” for film or “Apple” for computers) receive broader protection than weak, descriptive marks.
  • Channels of trade and buyer sophistication: Goods that move in the same marketing channels (e.g., online retail) are more likely to cause confusion. Moreover, marks targeting ordinary, low-involvement consumers are more susceptible to confusion than those aimed at specialized or sophisticated buyers. See, e.g., Citigroup, Inc. v. City Holding Co., 171 F. Supp. 2d 333, 349 (S.D.N.Y. 2001).
  • Evidence of actual confusion: Documented confusion supports likelihood of confusion, but its absence does not rule it out. See, e.g., Brookfield Comms., Inc. v. West Coast Entert. Corp., 174 F.3d 1036, 1050 (9th Cir. 1999).
  • Number and nature of similar marks in use: Widespread third-party use of similar marks on related goods can weaken protection for the prior mark and reduce confusion risk. Tektronix, Inc. v. Daktronics, Inc., 534 F.2d 915, 916 (CCPA 1976).
  • Applicant’s Intent: Knowledge of a prior mark when adopting a new mark increases likelihood of confusion. W.W.W. Pharmaceutical Co. v. Gillette Co., 984 F.2d 567, 575 (2d Cir 1993).

If search results reveal potentially conflicting marks, a trademark attorney can provide a full risk assessment, often concluding that low-risk conflicts can be navigated while high-risk ones may require rebranding to avoid costly disputes or willful infringement liability.

Other Key Considerations to Minimize Risk

Several practical steps can further reduce risk. First, clearance should be completed early, before finalizing branding or making significant marketing investments. Second, to the fullest extent possible, brand owners should favor inherently distinctive marks over descriptive ones, since they are easier to clear and easier to enforce. Third, documenting the search and decision-making process can be valuable if intent is later questioned in a willful infringement claim.

Finally, it’s important to recognize that trademark clearance does not eliminate all risk. That is rarely possible. The goal is informed risk management.  By understanding potential conflicts, businesses can select stronger marks, adjust branding strategies, or proceed with eyes wide open.
