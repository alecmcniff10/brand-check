USPTO Trademark Registration Series: Part 3 – Preparing and Filing Your Trademark Application

This is the third installment in a seven-part series on the USPTO trademark registration process. The first article introduced trademarks, and discussed when federal registration makes strategic sense.

Alec McNiff · 10 min read

This is the third installment in a seven-part series on the USPTO trademark registration process. The first article introduced trademarks, and discussed when federal registration makes strategic sense. The second article explored trademark clearance searches, including how to conduct them and interpret results to minimize risk. This article focuses on the next phase of registration: preparing and filing the trademark application.

Once a mark is cleared, applicants must assemble key elements, choose the appropriate filing basis, and submit through the USPTO’s online system. As of early 2026, the average time from filing to first examining action is around 5-6 months, with full registration (or abandonment) averaging 11-12 months. Getting the application right from the start is crucial to avoid delays, refusals, or abandonment. Moreover, surcharges fr incomplete applications or errors implemented by the USPTO in January 2025 can also add significant costs, making accuracy on the initial submission even more important.

This article covers key components of the filing process, from selecting a filing basis to submission tips, with practical guidance to navigate the process effectively.

Choosing the Filing Basis

The filing basis establishes the legal foundation for trademark rights and determines what evidence an applicant must provide. Under the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. § 1051 et seq.), U.S. applicants typically select either a domestic filing basis under Section 1 or a foreign-related basis under Sections 44 or 66. Selecting the wrong basis can result in refusal or require amendment, so the filing basis should align with the applicant’s current use of the mark and future business plans.

The two most common filing bases are:

  • Section 1(a) – Use in Commerce: This basis applies to marks already in use in interstate commerce. It requires submission of a specimen showing the mark as used on or in connection with the identified goods or services (e.g., a product label for “FitTrack” fitness trackers or a website screenshot for app services), along with the date of first use anywhere and the date of first use in commerce. Use must be a bona fide use in the ordinary course of trade and not use made solely to reserve trademark rights. This basis is appropriate when the applicant has launched the goods or services and can demonstrate commercial activity. It provides immediate constructive priority as of the filing date but limits protection to the specific goods or services in use at the time of filing.
  • Section 1(b) – Intent-to-Use (ITU): This basis is designed for pre-launch brands and allows filing based on a bona fide intent to use the mark in commerce. No specimen is required at filing. If use begins after filing but before the application is approved for publication, the applicant may file an Amendment to Allege Use (AAU) with evidence of use. After a Notice of Allowance issues, the applicant must file a Statement of Use (SOU) with evidence of use, or request an extension, within six months. Extensions may be requested for up to three years total. An ITU filing secures an early priority date based on constructive use from the filing date, which can be valuable for expanding businesses, such as a startup developing “FitTrack” software. An ITU application cannot register until use is proven, and failure to file a timely SOU or extension will result in abandonment.

Additional filing bases include:

  • Section 44 – Qualified Foreign Applicant: A foreign entity that owns a trademark application or registration in another country may rely on Section 44. Section 44(d) allows an applicant to claim priority based on a foreign application filed within the preceding six months, while Section 44(e) is based on an existing foreign registration. These bases do not require U.S. use at filing, although use must generally be shown later.
  • Section 66 – Extension of Protection under the Madrid Protocol: This basis permits an owner of an international trademark application or registration to extend protection to the United States through the Madrid Protocol, provided the applicant’s home country is a treaty member.

If multiple bases apply, such as actual use for some goods and intent-to-use for others, an applicant may claim more than one basis in a single application. Applicants should always verify eligibility, as misrepresenting intent or use can invalidate an application filed under Section 1(b).

Selecting Goods and Services

Accurately identifying goods and services is essential, as it defines the scope of protection. The USPTO uses the Nice Classification system, an international framework dividing trademarks into 45 classes (1-34 for goods, 35-45 for services). Overly broad or vague descriptions can lead to office actions requiring clarification or narrowing, while marks that are merely descriptive may face refusals under Section 2(e).

An applicant should begin with the USPTO’s Acceptable Identification of Goods and Services Manual (ID Manual), a free, searchable database of pre-approved descriptions. Using an identification directly from the ID Manual in the application can avoid additional fees. Applicants should search for terms related to their brand. For example, FitTrack could fall under Class 9 (“downloadable mobile applications for tracking fitness”) or Class 28 (“wearable activity trackers”). Descriptions should be precise, clear, and concise. For instance, rather than using a broad term like “health products,” applicants should specify “wearable electronic devices for monitoring physical activity.”

Several practical considerations can help applicants maximize the effectiveness of their goods and services descriptions:

  • An applicant should file in multiple classes if needed (e.g., software in Class 9 and related consulting in Class 42), understanding that each class incurs separate fees.
  • Indefinite or overly broad claims should be avoided; examiners often reject “kitchen sink” descriptions such as “all goods in Class 9.”
  • For ITU filings, descriptions should align with the applicant’s planned use to prevent the need for later amendments.
  • Future business expansions should be considered—registering broader but defensible identifications can reduce the need for supplemental filings.
  • Using the free-form text box instead of the ID Manual incurs a $200 surcharge per class, with an additional $200 per class for each 1,000-character group beyond the first 1,000.

Inaccurate identifications of goods and services frequently result in refusals, so it can be prudent to cross-reference the ID Manual’s pre-approved descriptions with examples from similar registrations.

Preparing Application Elements

A complete trademark application includes several core components. An applicant should gather these elements before filing to streamline the process.

  • Owner and Contact Details: The applicant, defined as the individual or entity that controls the nature or quality of the goods or services identified by the trademark, must be identified for registration. An applicant should provide the name, domicile address, email address, the type of legal entity, and citizenship or the state or nation under whose laws the entity is organized (e.g., a U.S. corporation must specify the state of incorporation). For partnerships, the names and citizenship of general partners should be included. The applicant need not be a U.S. citizen and may be any type of legal entity.
  • Attorney Information: If representation is required or chosen, the application should include the attorney’s name, postal and email address, and bar information (37 C.F.R. § 2.17(b)(3)). Foreign-domiciled applicants must appoint a qualified U.S.-licensed attorney to file and represent them before the USPTO (37 C.F.R. § 2.11).
  • Mark Drawing: The mark drawing depicts how the trademark appears. For standard character marks (plain words, e.g., “FitTrack”), applicants should submit text form for broad protection across fonts and styles. For special form marks (logos, designs, or marks with specific style, lettering, or color), a JPEG image in black-and-white or color (if claiming color) should be submitted. Specimens should not be included in the drawing; the depiction must be clear and match the intended use.
  • Specimen (for Section 1(a) only): For each class of goods or services, applicants must provide a specimen demonstrating actual use in commerce. Acceptable specimens for goods include tags, labels, or photographs showing the mark on the goods. For services, specimens may include advertisements, correspondence, or photographs showing the mark in connection with the services. The specimen must match the mark as filed and link directly to the goods or services; generic advertising or digital mock-ups are not acceptable and can lead to fraud claims.
  • Other Elements: Applicants should include translations for foreign words, descriptions of non-traditional marks (e.g., sounds), or disclaimers for unregistrable elements (e.g., disclaiming “Fit” in “FitTrack” to indicate no exclusive rights to that word). Additional requirements may apply if the mark is a collective or certification mark.
  • Verified Statement: The applicant (or an authorized representative under 37 C.F.R. § 2.193(e)(1)) must sign a verified statement, which may be done by declaration. The statement should affirm that, to the best of the applicant’s knowledge: the facts in the application are true; the applicant believes it is the owner of the mark; no other entity has the right to use the mark or a confusingly similar mark in commerce; if use-based, the mark is in use in commerce with the claimed goods or services; and if intent-to-use, the applicant has a bona fide intent to use the mark in commerce with the claimed goods or services. The application should be signed electronically, affirming accuracy under penalty of perjury.

Incomplete or missing elements often trigger office actions, which can delay examination by several months, adding unnecessary expenses.

Filing Options and Fees

All filings are online via the USPTO’s Trademark Electronic Application System (TEAS), accessible through the Trademark Center. Paper filings are no longer accepted except in very limited circumstances, which incur higher fees.

Effective January 18, 2025, the USPTO has consolidated the previous TEAS Plus and TEAS Standard options into a single base application fee of $350 per class for applications under Sections 1 and 44. This simplifies filing but may increase costs for some due to the elimination of the lower Plus tier. Additional surcharges may apply based on complexity:

  • $200 per class for using free-form text instead of ID Manual identifications.
  • $200 per affected class for each additional 1,000 characters beyond the first 1,000 in the free-form text box.
  • $100 per class for insufficient information (Sections 1 and 44).

Other fees include $150 per class for an amendment to allege use or statement of use, and $125 per class for a six-month extension for the statement of use. Filing fees are based on the number of classes plus any applicable surcharges, must be paid upfront, and are non-refundable. For a simple application, total costs can range from $350 to over $1,000, excluding attorney fees.

While individuals can file pro se, attorneys are recommended for complex cases to avoid pitfalls like improper classifications or responses to refusals. U.S.-domiciled applicants can self-represent, but foreign ones must use a U.S.-licensed attorney.

To receive a filing date (generally the effective date for priority or constructive use), ensure the application includes at a minimum: the applicant’s name, address, and email address; if applicable, the attorney’s name, address, and email address; a clear drawing of the mark; a listing of the goods or services; and a filing fee for at least one class (37 C.F.R. § 2.21). To avoid additional fees, comply with the requirements for a base application in 37 C.F.R. § 2.22.

Submission Tips

After an application is prepared, the applicant can take several practical steps to ensure a smooth filing process and reduce the risk of delays or errors: 

  • File as early as possible to secure priority, as delays increase the risk of intervening third-party filings.
  • Carefully review the application for errors before submission, and use the USPTO’s validation tools to identify missing or inconsistent information.
  • After filing, monitor the application’s status through the Trademark Status and Document Retrieval (TSDR) system.
  • Address any pre-examination notices from the USPTO promptly to avoid delays or abandonment.

Once an application is filed, the USPTO issues a filing receipt by email assigning a serial number and summarizing the submitted information. Upon receipt, the applicant should review the filing receipt for accuracy, retain it as evidence of the filing date in case questions arise, and promptly notify the USPTO of any discrepancies between the submitted application and the filing receipt. Corrections to errors in the original application must be made through a preliminary amendment rather than informal correspondence.

Key Considerations to Avoid Common Pitfalls

Several critical issues can derail a trademark application if not addressed properly. Common examples include:

  • Accurate Identifications: Overbroad identifications can increase the risk of Section 2(d) refusals for likelihood of confusion. Additionally, if the mark itself is descriptive, evidence of secondary meaning may be required to overcome a Section 2(e)(1) refusal. Using entries from the ID Manual can both avoid surcharges and strengthen the application.
  • Specimen Requirements: Specimens must show bona fide use, not merely promotional materials. Invalid specimens can result in amendments or abandonment.
  • Disclaimers: Applicants should proactively disclaim generic or descriptive elements to reinforce the application (e.g., disclaiming “Fit” if it is descriptive in FitTrack).
  • Risks of Incompleteness: Deficient applications may face suspension or abandonment after six months without response. Fraudulent statements, such as false claims of use, can invalidate registration. See 15 U.S.C. § 1064(3) 
  • Signature and Verification: The signer must have proper authority; misrepresentations can violate 18 U.S.C. § 1001.

Applicants should also consider post-filing monitoring for oppositions and align filings with business timelines, keeping in mind that while ITU applications provide flexibility, eventual use of the mark is required.
