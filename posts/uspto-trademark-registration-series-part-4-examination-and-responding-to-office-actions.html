USPTO Trademark Registration Series: Part 4 – Examination and Responding to Office Actions

Alec McNiff · 8 min read

Filing a trademark application is just the beginning of the registration process. After submission, each application is reviewed by the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) to determine whether it complies with statutory requirements and whether the mark is eligible for registration. This examination phase is where many applications encounter their first serious obstacles to registration. Strategic responses can make the difference between registration and abandonment.

What Happens After Filing

Once an application is filed, it enters a USPTO processing queue and is eventually assigned to an examining attorney. The initial examination typically occurs about four to five months after filing, though timing can vary depending on USPTO workload and filing basis.

The examining attorney conducts a substantive and procedural review of the application. This includes confirming that the application is complete, that the identification of goods or services is acceptable, and that the mark does not violate any statutory bars to registration. If the examining attorney identifies any issues, the USPTO issues an office action explaining the problems and what must be done to address them.

Importantly, receiving an office action is not unusual. More than half of all trademark applications receive an initial refusal or requirement, even for marks that ultimately proceed to registration.

Common Grounds for Refusal

Office actions generally fall into two categories: substantive refusals and procedural or technical issues. Substantive refusals challenge whether a mark is eligible for registration, while procedural or technical issues may arise from application errors, incomplete information, or missing signatures. Both types of refusals must be addressed within the response period.

Among substantive refusals, likelihood of confusion is the most common. Under Section 2(d) of the Lanham Act, the USPTO will refuse registration if the applied-for mark is confusingly similar to a prior registered or pending mark, considering factors such as similarity of the marks, relatedness of the goods or services, and overlap in trade channels.

Merely descriptive or geographically descriptive refusals arise when the mark directly describes a feature, purpose, quality, or geographic origin of the goods or services. These refusals often hinge on whether consumers would perceive the mark as conveying information rather than identifying source.

Genericness and failure-to-function refusals address marks that do not operate as trademarks at all. Generic terms name the product or service itself, such as attempting to register “Cold Brew Coffee” for cold brew coffee. Failure-to-function refusals often involve slogans or common phrases like “Best Beer in Town” that consumers perceive as promotional or informational rather than as indicators of source.

Ornamental refusals typically appear in apparel applications where a design or phrase is used primarily as decoration rather than as a source identifier. For example, a large slogan printed across the front of a T-shirt may be refused if it appears to be decorative messaging, while the same wording used on a hangtag or neck label is more likely to function as a trademark.

Each refusal requires a tailored response within three months of the office action, including both legal argument and factual support. Applicants may seek one three-month extension of the response deadline for a fee. 37 C.F.R. § 2.62(d). 

Understanding Non-Final and Final Office Actions

The first office action issued is usually non-final, meaning the applicant has an opportunity to respond and resolve the issues raised. The USPTO sets a strict six-month deadline from the mailing date of the office action, which includes an initial three-month period plus the optional three-month extension that the applicant can request for a fee. Missing this deadline results in abandonment of the application, with no extensions available.

If the examining attorney finds the response unpersuasive or incomplete, the USPTO may issue a final office action. A final action does not mean the application is dead, but it limits the applicant’s available response options. After receiving a final office action, applicants may still submit certain amendments, request reconsideration, or appeal the refusal to the Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB), as discussed below.

Strategy for Responding to an Office Action

An office action response is an organized submission that addresses all refusals and requirements raised by the USPTO. Responses typically include legal arguments, amendments to the application, and supporting evidence, as appropriate. Applicants preparing an effective office action response should typically do the following:

1. Identify each refusal and requirement separately. Office actions often raise multiple refusals or requirements, and the applicant must address each one individually. Overlooking even one issue can result in abandonment of the application. Applicants (or, more specifically, their attorneys) must carefully review the action and create a clear list of all substantive and procedural points to ensure nothing is missed.

2. Classify each issue as procedural or substantive. Procedural or technical issues, such as clarifying the identification of goods and services, providing disclaimers, correcting minor application errors, or submitting missing information, are generally easier to resolve, typically by simple amendments. Substantive refusals, such as likelihood of confusion, descriptiveness, or genericness, require legal analysis and evidence to overcome. Distinguishing between the two helps prioritize resources and plan an effective response.

3. Decide how to address each refusal. For each refusal or requirement, applicants should determine the most effective approach to overcome the issue. A combination of amendments and legal arguments often provides the strongest path to registration.

Procedural issues or minor refinements can frequently be resolved by amending the application. Common amendments include narrowing the identification of goods or services to reduce overlap with cited marks, adding disclaimers for descriptive terms, or adjusting the mark’s presentation. While narrowing may limit the scope of protection, it can substantially increase the likelihood of registration and may also strengthen substantive legal arguments.

Substantive refusals, such as likelihood of confusion, descriptiveness, or failure-to-function, typically require well-supported legal arguments. These may involve distinguishing the applied-for mark from cited marks, challenging the examining attorney’s interpretation of descriptiveness, or demonstrating that the mark functions as a source identifier. Arguments should rely on USPTO precedent and be supported with evidence whenever possible.

For descriptive marks, applicants may also rely on acquired distinctiveness under Section 2(f) to show that consumers associate the mark with a single source. Evidence supporting a Section 2(f) claim can include length and exclusivity of use, sales figures, advertising expenditures, media recognition, and consumer declarations. While Section 2(f) claims can be highly effective, weak or insufficient evidence may undermine the overall application, so careful assessment is essential.

By considering amendments, arguments, and acquired distinctiveness together, applicants can craft a response that maximizes the chance of overcoming refusals while preserving as much trademark protection as possible.

4. Submit evidence where appropriate. Evidence may include third-party registrations, marketplace evidence, dictionary definitions, consumer perception evidence, or declarations. Evidence can be critical to overcoming substantive refusals. Evidence might include

  • Third-party registrations to show similar marks coexist without confusion.
  • Marketplace evidence demonstrating how the mark is actually used in commerce.
  • Dictionary or industry definitions supporting non-descriptive or suggestive character.
  • Consumer perception surveys or declarations showing how the mark is perceived in the relevant marketplace.
  • Specimen examples showing the mark in actual use.

5. Follow formal submission requirements. All responses must comply with USPTO rules, including filing through the Trademark Electronic Application System (TEAS) and meeting the six-month deadline from the office action mailing date (assuming the applicant obtained a three-month extension). Responses should be organized, clearly labeled, and include both legal argument and evidence in support of each point.

Requesting Reconsideration and Appealing a Final Refusal

If a final office action maintains a refusal, applicants can request reconsideration from the examining attorney, appeal to the TTAB, or both. 

A request for reconsideration allows the applicant to submit new arguments or evidence not previously presented, but it must be filed before the deadline for filing a notice of appeal. The request is submitted using the USPTO’s Request for Reconsideration after Final Office Action Form. Applicants should include all evidence they might rely on in a potential appeal to ensure the record is complete, since the TTAB generally does not consider new evidence introduced for the first time on appeal. Filing a request for reconsideration does not extend the deadline for filing a notice of appeal or other response to the final action.

Alternatively, an appeal to the TTAB is authorized by the Lanham Act and allows applicants to challenge a final refusal on legal grounds. The appeal process begins with filing a notice of appeal and paying the required fee for each class in the application. Notices must be filed through the USPTO’s Electronic System for Trademark Trials and Appeals (ESTTA). The appeal brief is then due within 60 days of filing the notice. Appeals are typically most appropriate for legal issues, such as likelihood of confusion or descriptiveness, rather than factual disputes, and applicants should weigh the potential cost, complexity, and likelihood of success before proceeding. Failure to timely file a notice of appeal and pay the fee results in abandonment, though under certain circumstances, the applicant may file a petition to revive the application.

Applicants may also file a notice of appeal concurrently with a request for reconsideration. In this scenario, the application is referred to the TTAB, which acknowledges the appeal and suspends further proceedings while the examining attorney reviews the request for reconsideration. If the examining attorney maintains the refusal, the applicant may continue with the appeal, provided the notice of appeal was filed timely. This approach allows applicants to preserve the option of appealing while still seeking to resolve the refusal directly with the examining attorney.

Conclusion

USPTO examination is where trademark applications are evaluated. Success depends on understanding the examining attorney’s concerns, responding within strict deadlines, and making informed strategic choices. Because initial refusals are common, receiving an office action should be viewed as a routine part of the trademark registration process, not a sign of failure. With careful analysis and a disciplined response strategy, many office action issues can be resolved, often leading to approval and eventual publication of the mark for opposition.
