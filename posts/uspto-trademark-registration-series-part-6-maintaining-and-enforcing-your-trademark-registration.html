USPTO Trademark Registration Series: Part 6 – Maintaining and Enforcing Your Trademark Registration

Obtaining a federal trademark registration from the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) is a meaningful milestone, but it is not the finish line.

Alec McNiff · 6 min read

Obtaining a federal trademark registration from the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) is a meaningful milestone, but it is not the finish line. Registration creates powerful legal advantages only if the mark is continuously used and actively protected. Failure to maintain or enforce a registration can result in cancellation, erosion of rights, and the loss of benefits such as nationwide priority and enhanced enforcement remedies. This article outlines the mandatory and optional post-registration filings, enforcement strategies, and practical safeguards that help ensure a trademark remains a durable business asset.

Required Post-Registration Filings

To keep a registration active, owners must file specific documents with the USPTO at set intervals. These filings are mandatory, fee-based, and generally must be submitted electronically through TEAS. Registrants domiciled outside the United States must be represented by a U.S.-licensed attorney.

Declaration of Continued Use or Excusable Nonuse Under Section 8 or 71

The Declaration (or Affidavit) of Continued Use or Excusable Nonuse confirms that the mark remains in use in commerce or that any temporary nonuse is legally excusable, preventing cancellation for abandonment. Most trademark registrations rely on Section 8 of the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. § 1058) for filing this declaration, which applies to standard U.S. federal trademark registrations. In contrast, Section 71 (15 U.S.C. § 1141k) is used specifically for international trademark registrations that have been extended to the U.S. under the Madrid Protocol.

Filings are due within one year before the end of:

  • The sixth year after registration.
  • The tenth year after registration and every ten years thereafter.

A six-month grace period is available for an additional fee. Missing the deadline (including the grace period) results in automatic cancellation (though common law rights remain).

The declaration must:

  • Identify the registration by number.
  • Be filed by the current owner.
  • Include a verified statement attesting to use (or excusable nonuse) in commerce during the period.
  • Specify covered goods or services; delete or claim excusable nonuse for those not in use to avoid deletion.
  • Contain a warning against false statements under 18 U.S.C. § 1001.

Unless excusable nonuse is claimed for all goods or services in a class, the owner must submit at least one specimen per class showing actual use in commerce. The specimen need not be visually identical to the registered mark, but it must create the same commercial impression.

Excusable nonuse is narrowly construed. It applies only where use has been temporarily discontinued due to circumstances beyond the owner’s control, coupled with a bona fide intent to resume use. Examples may include interruptions caused by natural disasters, trade embargoes, government restrictions, supply chain shutdowns, or serious illness of an indispensable owner. Nonuse driven by ordinary business decisions, rebranding, marketing strategy shifts, lack of demand, or use of the mark on different goods or services does not qualify. When excusable nonuse is claimed, the owner must explain when use stopped, why it stopped, what steps are being taken to resume use, and when use is expected to restart. Conclusory or unsupported statements are insufficient and can jeopardize the registration.

Because Section 8 and 71 declarations are verified statements of use, they are subject to the USPTO’s post-registration audit program. Registrations with broad identifications or questionable specimens may be audited, requiring additional proof of use. Failure to respond or provide adequate evidence can result in deletion of goods or cancellation of the registration.

Renewal Application (Section 9)

Trademark registrations must be renewed every ten years. A Section 9 renewal is filed during the final year of each ten-year term, with a six-month grace period available for an added fee. By contrast, international registrations renew through the International Bureau rather than the USPTO.

In practice, the Section 9 renewal is typically filed together with the required Section 8 or 71 declaration due at the same time.

Optional Post-Registration Measures

Declaration of Incontestability (Section 15)

After five consecutive years of continuous use following registration, an owner may file a Section 15 Declaration to claim incontestable status, which limits certain validity challenges, including claims that the mark is merely descriptive. The declaration must state that: (1) the mark has been in continuous use for five years, and (2) there have been no final adverse decisions or pending proceedings affecting the registration. Although the USPTO does not substantively examine Section 15 filings, incontestability can be tested in enforcement or cancellation proceedings.

Corrections, Amendments, and Ownership Updates

Errors in a registration should be corrected promptly. The USPTO will correct its own errors without charge and owner errors for a fee if made in good faith and without materially altering the registration. Material changes generally require cancellation and refiling.

Owners may either delete unused goods or services or make limited, non-material amendments, but they cannot expand the scope of the registration or alter the mark’s commercial impression.

Assignments should be recorded with the USPTO to ensure accurate ownership records and avoid complications during enforcement or renewal.

Monitoring and Enforcing Your Trademark

A federal registration does not prevent infringement on its own; owners must actively enforce their rights to preserve the mark’s distinctiveness and value. Failure to address unauthorized use, such as infringement, dilution, or generic misuse, can weaken the mark, expose the owner to liability, or limit future legal remedies. Allowing uncontrolled use, including licensing without quality controls, can lead to partial abandonment or diminished enforceability. Consistent policing deters potential infringers and strengthens rights over time, supporting claims of acquired distinctiveness or fame in legal proceedings.

Effective monitoring combines both external and internal measures. Professional watch services can track new USPTO applications, domain registrations, social media activity, online marketplaces, and other potential sources of confusion. Internally, employees should be encouraged to promptly report suspected misuse, including sound-alike marks, lookalike designs, or generic references to the mark (e.g., using it as a verb or noun). Unchecked misuse may eventually render a mark generic or create defenses like acquiescence in court.

When potential infringement is identified, consult qualified counsel to evaluate options. Responses may include sending cease-and-desist letters, filing oppositions or cancellation proceedings at the Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB), or pursuing federal litigation to secure injunctions, monetary damages, and, in exceptional cases, attorneys’ fees and costs.

Owners should always carefully manage licensing or third-party use of the mark. For instance, agreements should include approval processes, quality control obligations, and termination rights to preserve the mark’s integrity and avoid jeopardizing its enforceability.

Maintaining comprehensive records is also essential for enforcement and demonstrating the mark’s ongoing value. This includes specimens of product packaging and advertising, sales data, market research on consumer recognition, and evidence of promotional activities. Such documentation supports priority and continuous use claims in adversarial proceedings and USPTO maintenance filings, helping defend against cancellation for abandonment or nonuse.

By treating monitoring and enforcement as ongoing responsibilities rather than occasional tasks, owners ensure their trademarks remain strong, distinctive, and valuable business assets.

International Protection Through the Madrid Protocol

For businesses operating internationally, the Madrid Protocol allows U.S. registrants to extend protection to multiple member countries through a single filing. These extensions remain dependent on the U.S. registration for the first five years, making domestic maintenance especially important.

Practical Safeguards

Several administrative practices can reduce compliance risk over time:

  • Calendar and docket all deadlines to avoid accidental cancellation
  • Maintain use evidence for each listed good or service
  • Expect audits if identifications are broad or specimens appear questionable
  • Use USPTO email reminders and deposit accounts to reduce administrative risk

Conclusion

Trademark protection does not end at registration. Ongoing use, accurate filings, and active enforcement are essential to preserving rights and avoiding cancellation. Owners who treat post-registration maintenance as a core compliance function, rather than an afterthought, place their brands in a far stronger position over the long term.
