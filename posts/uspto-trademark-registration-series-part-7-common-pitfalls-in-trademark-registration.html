USPTO Trademark Registration Series: Part 7 – Common Pitfalls in Trademark Registration

We conclude this series on trademark registration by examining where otherwise viable applications often go wrong. Many applicants encounter refusals or abandon applications due to preventable mistakes.

Alec McNiff · 3 min read

We conclude this series on trademark registration by examining where otherwise viable applications often go wrong. Many applicants encounter refusals or abandon applications due to preventable mistakes. Understanding these recurring pitfalls can save time and reduce costs and frustration. This article summarizes common errors, explains how applicants can overcome certain refusals, and flags emerging risks such as trademark scams.

Frequent Errors That Lead to Refusals or Delays

A large percentage of trademark applications encounter substantive or procedural issues during examination. According to one source citing USPTO data, only about 54 percent of applications filed in 2023 ultimately matured to registration, meaning nearly half faced refusals, abandonment, or other obstacles. Many of these outcomes result from avoidable oversights, including:

  • Inadequate pre-filing searches: Skipping a comprehensive clearance search remains one of the most common and costly mistakes. Without evaluating existing registrations and pending applications, applicants often face likelihood-of-confusion refusals under Section 2(d) of the Lanham Act, based on similarities in appearance, sound, meaning, or commercial impression.
  • Selection of weak or barred marks: Generic, merely descriptive, or geographically descriptive terms routinely trigger refusals. A term that directly names the goods or services, such as “Computer” for computers, can never function as a trademark and is not registrable under any circumstances.
  • Incorrect or incomplete application information: Errors in ownership are particularly dangerous. Listing the wrong owner, such as an individual instead of the controlling business entity, is frequently fatal and cannot always be corrected without refiling. Overbroad or inaccurate identifications of goods and services, incorrect classifications, and missing required information also delay or derail applications.
  • Defective specimens: For use-based filings, specimens must show the mark used in commerce as a source identifier, not merely as decoration or in mock-ups. Common refusals arise when applicants submit labels, screenshots, or promotional materials that fail to demonstrate trademark use.
  • Missed deadlines: Failure to respond to Office Actions within six months, file Statements of Use for intent-to-use applications, or submit post-registration maintenance filings results in abandonment, often without recourse.

Each of these mistakes increases the likelihood of delays, additional fees, or the need to start over entirely.

Refusals in Practice and How Some Are Overcome

Refusals are not the exception. Most applications receive at least one Office Action, and strategic responses can make the difference between abandonment and registration.

For example, the Washington Commanders’ trademark application initially received a Section 2(d) refusal in 2022 based on similarities to existing “Commanders” marks. Through targeted arguments distinguishing the marks and the relevant goods and services, the refusal was ultimately withdrawn and the application proceeded to registration.

Other refusals are resolved through evidentiary showings. Descriptive marks may be registered under Section 2(f) if the applicant can prove acquired distinctiveness through long-term use, advertising expenditures, and consumer recognition. Specimen refusals are frequently cured by submitting substitute evidence that clearly shows real-world use, such as product packaging or properly structured website displays.

However, not all refusals are overcome. For instance, generic marks rarely succeed, and willful errors can lead to permanent bars to registration.

No Guarantees and Fraud Risks

Trademark registration is never guaranteed. Even strong applications may face opposition or unexpected refusals. Fraud risks are real and severe. False specimens, inaccurate use claims, or misrepresentations can invalidate a registration years later.

Applicants should also remain alert to trademark scams. The USPTO regularly warns about imposters sending fake notices that mimic official correspondence and demand immediate payment, often via wire transfer, gift cards, or payment apps. Legitimate USPTO communications never require urgent payment or threaten immediate loss of rights without formal process. Application status should always be verified through the USPTO’s Trademark Status and Document Retrieval system.

Final Takeaway

Trademark registration is a powerful brand protection tool, but it rewards preparation and discipline. Avoidable mistakes remain the leading cause of refusals and abandonment. By understanding common pitfalls, engaging qualified counsel when appropriate, and staying current on procedural changes, applicants can materially improve their odds of securing durable trademark rights. For most businesses, informed strategy at the outset is the most cost-effective decision they can make.
