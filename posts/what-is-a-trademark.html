Understanding Trademarks and How They Differ from Copyrights and Patents

Intellectual property law protects different kinds of creations in different ways. A common source of confusion is the distinction between trademarks, copyrights, and patents. This article explains, at a basic level, what a trademark is and why it’s fundamentally different from copyrights and patents.

Trademarks

A trademark is any word, name, symbol, sound, color, or smell that identifies the source of goods or services and distinguishes them from others. The legal definition appears in Section 45 of the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. § 1127), but the general idea is much simpler: a trademark tells the consumer, “This came from me.”

Examples of trademarks include the word STARBUCKS on a cup of coffee, the Nike swoosh on a sneaker, or the distinctive blue box from Tiffany & Co. Consumers instantly connect each of these to a single source, and trademark law exists to protect that connection.

Contrasting Trademarks With Copyrights and Patents

Copyright and patent law, on the other hand, serve entirely different purposes. Copyright protects original works of authorship that are fixed in a tangible medium: novels, songs, photographs, software code, architectural drawings, and the like. The moment an author finishes writing a story or a programmer saves a new piece of code, copyright protection automatically attaches to that work. The primary goal of copyright protection is to prevent unauthorized copying and to give creators control over reproduction, distribution, public performance, and derivative works.

Patent law protects inventions: new and useful processes, machines, manufactures, or compositions of matter (as well as new ornamental designs). A patent grants its owner the right to exclude others from making, using, or selling the invention for a limited time. Unlike copyright, patent protection is never automatic; it requires a rigorous examination by the United States Patent and Trademark Office.

The differences in duration between trademark protection, on the one hand, and copyrights and patents, on the other, are key. Copyright protection lasts for the life of the author plus seventy years (or ninety-five to one hundred twenty years for works made for hire). Patents expire after twenty years from the filing date.

Trademarks, however, can last indefinitely as long as the owner continues to use the mark, renews its registration (if registered), and defends it against infringement or dilution. Trademark protection can last forever because its sole purpose is to prevent ongoing consumer confusion in the marketplace. As long as the mark is actively used and continues to identify a single source, society still benefits from that protection. Copyright and patent rights, however, are temporary monopolies granted for a limited time to incentivize creation and disclosure of new works and inventions, after which they must enter the public domain so everyone can freely build upon them.

How Trademark Rights Arise

Trademark rights can arise in two ways. First, simply by using a mark in commerce, a business can acquire common-law rights in the geographic area(s) where the business actually uses the mark. No filing is required, and the rights continue as long as the business keeps selling under that mark and the mark does not become generic.

Second, a business can apply for federal registration with the United States Patent and Trademark Office. Federal registration requires continued use as well. Once the registration issues, the owner enjoys nationwide priority, a legal presumption of validity after five years of continuous registration, and powerful additional remedies in court. The owner must renew the registration every ten years thereafter, but as long as the owner continues to use the mark, renews its registration, and defends it against infringement and genericide, the owner can maintain the registration indefinitely. 

Conclusion

A trademark, at its essence, is about source identification and consumer protection. It is a legal mechanism that gives consumers confidence that the product or service they are buying comes from the source they expect. Moreover, it gives businesses the exclusive right to that trust.

Unlike copyrights and patents, which expire so that creative works and inventions can eventually belong to everyone, trademarks can last forever. As long as the mark continues to function as a reliable indicator of source and the owner actively uses and defends it, the law recognizes that consumer recognition and the goodwill tied to a brand have no natural expiration.
