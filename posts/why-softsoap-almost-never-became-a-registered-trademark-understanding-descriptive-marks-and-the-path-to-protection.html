Why “Softsoap” Almost Never Became a Registered Trademark: Understanding Descriptive Marks and the Path to Protection

When creating brand names, many entrepreneurs wish to use words that describe what the product does, such as “Pure Water,” or “Soft Soap.” Although this impulse is understandable, it can cause issues when applying for federal trademark registration.

Alec McNiff · 5 min read

When creating brand names, many entrepreneurs wish to use words that describe what the product does, such as “Pure Water,” or “Soft Soap.” Although this impulse is understandable, it can cause issues when applying for federal trademark registration.

The Five Categories of Marks

The United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) and federal courts evaluate trademarks on a spectrum of distinctiveness. See, e.g., Abercrombie & Fitch Co. v. Hunting World, Inc., 537 F.2d 4, 9 (2d Cir. 1976).  Marks are placed into one of the following categories:

  1. Generic: the common name of the product itself (e.g., “Water” for bottled water). Generic marks are never protectable.
  2. Descriptive: immediately tells the consumer what the product is or does, without requiring any imagination, by conveying an ingredient, quality, characteristic, feature, function, or purpose of the goods (e.g., “Softsoap,” “Crunchy Cereal”). Descriptive marks are eligible for registration only after acquiring “secondary meaning,” as discussed below.
  3. Suggestive: requires a step of imagination to connect the mark to the goods (e.g., “Coppertone” for sunscreen). Suggestive marks are considered inherently distinctive and protectable.
  4. Arbitrary: an existing word applied in an unrelated context (e.g., “Apple” for computers). Arbitrary marks are also inherently distinctive and receive strong protection.
  5. Fanciful: invented words (e.g., “Kodak,” “Xerox”). Fanciful marks, which are also inherently distinctive, are the strongest marks.

Importantly, descriptive marks are a border case between unprotectable generic terms and inherently distinctive marks. From the USPTO’s perspective, no single seller should be allowed to monopolize ordinary language that competitors need to simply describe their own identical or similar products. Allowing one company to immediately own “Soft Soap” would make it nearly impossible for competitors to tell consumers their soap is also soft. Thus, descriptive marks are initially refused registration unless and until they acquire secondary meaning—i.e., consumers have come to recognize the term as identifying a single brand rather than just describing the product.

This principle explains Soft Soap’s seven-year fight to secure federal registration, discussed below.

The Softsoap Saga

In 1980, Minnetonka, Inc. launched SOFTSOAP, the first mass-market liquid hand soap in a pump dispenser. The product quickly achieved massive sales through heavy advertising and exclusive pump supply control. 

Prior to the product’s launch, the USPTO refused Minnetonka’s trademark application on the ground that “Softsoap” was merely descriptive (or highly descriptive bordering on generic) of liquid soap. See In re Minnetonka, Inc., 212 USPQ 772, 782 (TTAB 1981). After filing a civil action pursuant to Section 21(b), Minnetonka refiled its application in December 1983 with extensive proof of secondary meaning, including affidavits, consumer surveys, advertising materials and evidence of exclusive use. On April 9, 1987, the Trademark Trial and Appeal Board (TTAB) reversed the refusal, concluding that while highly descriptive, “SOFTSOAP” had come to identify Minnetonka as the single source in consumers’ minds. The mark registered on the Principal Register on September 22, 1987 (U.S. Reg. No. 1,457,886). Thus, this case is a classic example of how descriptive terms can achieve full protection through strong evidence of acquired distinctiveness.

Many now-familiar marks followed a similar path. For instance, American Airlines began as geographically descriptive, simply indicating an airline operating in or from the United States. But through decades of exclusive use and nationwide advertising since the 1930s, consumers eventually came to associate the mark solely with one carrier, allowing it to acquire secondary meaning and secure strong federal protection. Sharp, the television brand, was originally descriptive of picture quality: a “sharp” image. But over years of consistent branding and market presence, the term shifted in consumers’ minds to identify products from Sharp Corporation specifically, transforming it into a protectable source indicator. Finally, Holiday Inn, the hotel chain, started as a purely descriptive phrase suggesting lodgings suitable for holidays or vacations. Persistent nationwide use, iconic signage, and heavy promotion starting in the 1950s built exclusive recognition, enabling the mark to acquire secondary meaning and achieve registration on the Principal Register.

These examples illustrate that descriptive marks, though initially weak and often refused registration, can acquire secondary meaning through extensive use, advertising, and proven consumer recognition. This process ultimately grants them the same strong protection enjoyed by inherently distinctive marks.

Practical Advice for Brands

  1. Prioritize suggestive, arbitrary, or fanciful marks from the start. Names like “Dove” (suggestive of gentleness and purity for soap) or “Apple” (arbitrary for computers) are inherently distinctive and typically register quickly without extra evidence or cost. In contrast, descriptive terms like “Softsoap” face initial refusals and can require years of use, substantial proof of secondary meaning, additional filings, and higher legal expenses.
  1. If a descriptive name is unavoidable, plan for the long road to full protection. Budget time and resources for extensive marketing, consistent and substantially exclusive use, and gathering robust evidence of acquired distinctiveness (e.g., sales data, advertising expenditures, consumer surveys, and declarations). This evidence is often essential to overcome a Section 2(f) refusal and move the mark to the Principal Register.
  1. Use dual or composite branding to maximize immediate protection. Pair a descriptive term with a distinctive house mark, logo, or stylized element (e.g., OWALA FreeSip, STANLEY Quencher, or DOVE with its distinctive swan logo). The distinctive component can often register right away, providing enforceable rights while the descriptive portion builds secondary meaning over time.

Key Takeaway

Descriptive marks are not barred from protection forever, but they begin on the weakest footing and face significant hurdles to registration on the Principal Register. The USPTO requires substantial evidence that the term has acquired secondary meaning and now functions as a true source identifier in consumers’ minds. Proving this often takes years of exclusive use, heavy advertising, and market dominance, all before the mark can be registered.

The Softsoap saga, along with familiar brands like American Airlines, Sharp, and Holiday Inn, demonstrate that this transformation is possible with persistence and marketplace success. However, these stories also serve as a powerful reminder that the safest, fastest, and most cost-effective route to immediate trademark protection almost always lies in choosing a suggestive, arbitrary, or fanciful name that sparks a bit of imagination from the very start.
