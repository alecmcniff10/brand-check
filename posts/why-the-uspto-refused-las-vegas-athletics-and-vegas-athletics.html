Why the USPTO Refused “Las Vegas Athletics” and “Vegas Athletics”

After announcing their relocation to Nevada, the Oakland Athletics (now the Las Vegas Athletics) looked to secure trademark protection for names reflecting their new home.

Alec McNiff · 5 min read

After announcing their relocation to Nevada, the Oakland Athletics (now the Las Vegas Athletics) looked to secure trademark protection for names reflecting their new home. Among the filings were applications for “Las Vegas Athletics” and “Vegas Athletics” covering a broad range of apparel and entertainment services. On December 29, 2025, the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) issued a non-final refusal, concluding that both marks are primarily geographically descriptive under Section 2(e)(2) of the Lanham Act.

The refusal raises the question: if “Las Vegas Athletics” cannot be registered, how do teams across every major league manage to secure federal registrations for city-based names?

The USPTO’s Refusal 

The examining attorney’s refusal closely followed the three-part test set out in TMEP §1210.01(a) for determining whether a mark is primarily geographically descriptive.

First, the primary significance of the mark must be a generally known geographic location. Here, both “Las Vegas” and “Vegas” satisfied that requirement. Commonly used geographic nicknames are treated as legally equivalent to the place itself, and the Office relied on dictionary and reference evidence confirming that “Vegas” is a well-recognized shorthand for Las Vegas.

Second, the goods or services must originate in the place identified. The applicant effectively conceded that element once it confirmed that the team would relocate and that its goods and services would be rendered from Las Vegas.

Third, consumers must be likely to believe that the goods or services originate in that location, creating a goods-place or services-place association. When a well-known location appears in a mark and the goods or services originate there, the law presumes a goods-place or services-place association, and no survey evidence is required. That presumption applied cleanly here.

Having concluded that the mark met all three elements of the geographic descriptiveness test, the outcome of the applications turned on the remaining element of the marks: “Athletics”.

“Athletics” Is Too Descriptive

Rather than mitigating the geographic significance of “Las Vegas,” the term “Athletics” only made the situation worse. Citing dictionary definitions and third-party usage establishing that consumers understand “athletics” to refer broadly to sports activities and sportswear, the examining attorney concluded that “athletics” is generic or, at a minimum, highly descriptive for sports teams, athletic apparel, and sports-related entertainment services. As a result, consumers would see the marks as saying exactly what they are and where they come from: athletic goods and services based in Las Vegas. There was no separate commercial impression, no imaginative leap for the consumer to make, and nothing in the mark to shift consumers’ focus away from geography.

That conclusion also undermined the applicant’s attempt to rely on prior registrations to establish acquired distinctiveness. The Office explained that because the marks are highly descriptive, Section 2(f) requires particularly strong evidence that consumers view the wording as a source identifier. Simply owning other registrations containing the word “Athletics” did not meet that burden, especially where those registrations involved different wording or stylization and therefore were not legal equivalents of the applied-for marks.

Why Other Sports Teams Clear This Hurdle

This raises the question of how other marks, including the Kansas City Chiefs or Chicago Bulls, are able to obtain registration. The answer lies in the fact that trademark law evaluates marks as a whole, and in marks like the Kansas City Chiefs or Chicago Bulls, the non-geographic element dominates the overall commercial impression.

Section 2(e)(2) does not prohibit geographic terms. It prohibits marks whose primary significance to consumers is geographic. 15 U.S.C. §§ 1052(e)(2) and (3). The addition of a distinctive element can shift the primary significance of a composite mark away from geography. Thus, a mark is not primarily geographically descriptive if the non-geographic portion creates a separate commercial impression that consumers perceive as source-identifying rather than informational.

“Kansas City Chiefs” illustrates this principle neatly. Kansas City is a well-known location, and the team’s services unquestionably originate there. But consumers do not understand the mark to mean “football services from Kansas City.” They understand it to mean a specific NFL franchise. The city name provides context while the nickname “Chiefs” does the trademark work. It is neither generic nor descriptive of football services, and it functions as the arbitrary identifier consumers actually remember.

That same dynamic applies across professional sports. “Chicago Bulls,” “Boston Red Sox,” and similar constructions pair geography with a non-descriptive nickname that overwhelms the geographic meaning. In those cases, geography remains part of the mark, but it does not shape the consumer’s primary understanding of it.

“Las Vegas Athletics,” however, fails because it does not follow that model. “Athletics” does not function as a distinctive nickname. It describes the very services being offered. So, when paired with a city name, it does not create a separate source-identifying impression. It simply confirms the geographic origin.

“Las Vegas A’s” Unlikely to Face the Same Fate

This framework also explains why “Las Vegas A’s” would be more likely to avoid a Section 2(e)(2) refusal. Like most team nicknames, “A’s” functions as a longstanding source identifier rather than a description of athletic services. When combined with “Las Vegas,” the mark begins to resemble the standard city-plus-nickname structure trademark law has long accepted. Geography remains part of the mark, but it no longer defines the mark’s primary significance.

That distinction does not guarantee registration, but it materially changes the analysis. Any refusal would require the USPTO to argue that geography still dominates consumer perception despite the presence of a recognized team identifier, which is a much harder case to make.

Conclusion

Trademark law does not prohibit geographic branding. It prohibits geographic branding that substitutes location for identity. Professional sports teams succeed in registering city-based marks not because the USPTO is indulgent, but because their marks tell consumers who the source is, not merely where it is located.

That distinction makes the difference  between a registrable sports trademark and refusal under Section 2(e)(2).
